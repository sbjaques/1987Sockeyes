# Matt Hervey - A California Kid on the Blueline

*A long-form biography of the 1987 Richmond Sockeyes defenceman who fought his way to the NHL and later changed his name to Matt Block.*

---

## 1. Lead - Second Shift, First Impression

In September 1987, a twenty-year-old walk-on from Whittier, California climbed over the boards for his second shift at the Winnipeg Jets' NHL training camp and, in a sequence burned into team memory, immediately "smacked Doug Smail" - one of the fastest, most respected Jets veterans. Laurie Boschman promptly vaulted off the bench to answer for his teammate. Matt Hervey, uninvited, undrafted, and unknown to most of the men in the rink, had just introduced himself.

"You get noticed that way," Moncton Hawks head coach Rick Bowness told the Telegraph-Journal a few months later, recounting the story as if it already belonged to league folklore (Telegraph-Journal, March 4 1988, image 1111187087). It was the Matt Hervey thesis statement: if you will not hand me a spot, I will take one.

Eighteen months earlier he had been a junior defenceman in Lethbridge, Alberta, tumbling out of a bench-clearing melee against the Medicine Hat Tigers (captured on the January 18, 1986 game film that still circulates among fight-video collectors). A year before that he had been a teenager standing on a Southern California roadside with his thumb out, hoping British Columbia would swallow him whole. And in the seventeen games between those moments and this one, he had been the finest defenceman on a team that nobody outside the British Columbia Junior Hockey League had any business taking seriously - the 1986-87 Richmond Sockeyes, the Centennial Cup champions of Canadian Junior A hockey.

This is the story of how he got from that hitchhiking moment in Los Angeles to an NHL sweater on Causeway Street, why his coaches kept telling anyone who would listen that he was the glue of every team he played for, and how - somewhere along the way - the name Matt Hervey gave way to the name Matt Block.

---

## 2. SoCal Origins

The paper record is consistent on the essentials and murky on the particulars. Matt Hervey was born in 1966 in Whittier, California, a working-class suburb south-east of Los Angeles more associated with Richard Nixon than with Canadian junior hockey. Hockeydb lists him as 5-foot-11, 205 pounds, a right-handed shot. His Sockeyes-era bio file in the team archive places the birthdate as May 16, 1966; other sources have circulated December 17, 1966. The discrepancy has never been resolved in print.

What is beyond dispute is the shape of his childhood. In November 1989, by then a third-year AHL defenceman in Moncton, Hervey sat down with Times-Transcript reporter Dwayne Tingley for a front-page feature that broke his own silence on the subject.

> "It wasn't a good situation. If I had stayed in L.A., I'd probably be working construction somewhere with an expensive drug habit to support."
> - Matt Hervey, Times-Transcript, November 10, 1989 (image 1107764485)

The paper reported that as a fifteen-year-old he had hitchhiked north from a "broken home" in Los Angeles, stopping at last in Langley, British Columbia, a Fraser Valley town he had never previously heard of. He went there to play hockey. There was no one back home who was going to talk him out of it, and there was no one in Langley waiting for him.

By 1989 Hervey and three Hawks teammates had begun speaking publicly to New Brunswick schoolchildren about substance abuse - unusual advocacy for a twenty-three-year-old minor-league defenceman whose professional reputation was built on fists. He had seen, up close, what the alternative looked like.

## 3. Langley, Victoria, Lethbridge - The Junior Years

The first hockey trace is 1982-83, age sixteen, with the Langley Eagles of the British Columbia Junior Hockey League: 53 games, 9 goals, 15 assists, 162 penalty minutes. The Eagles were the natural landing spot for the runaway Californian - a BCJHL club in the town where he had stepped off the highway - and the stat line already shows the player he would be for the next fifteen years. Offense from the blueline in useful doses; physical punishment out of all proportion to the scoring.

The performance was enough to earn him a promotion to major junior. In 1983-84 he played 61 games for the Victoria Cougars of the Western Hockey League (4-19-23, 89 PIM); the following season, after 14 games in Victoria, he was moved to the Lethbridge Broncos, where he finished the 1984-85 campaign with 54 games and 88 penalty minutes. Lethbridge kept him. He spent the entire 1985-86 season there - 60 games, 9 goals, 17 assists, 110 penalty minutes, plus 10 playoff games - and on the night of January 18, 1986, against the Medicine Hat Tigers, he was at the centre of a bench-clearing brawl that still survives on tape as part of the small but devoted genre of pre-internet hockey fight collection.

When Hervey arrived in Richmond in the middle of the 1986-87 season, he was not a kid with potential. He was a hardened twenty-year-old defenceman with four junior seasons behind him who had outgrown the WHL twice. Times Colonist, noting his arrival for the Abbott Cup, would identify him precisely - "a former member of the Victoria Cougars in major junior hockey" (April 28, 1987).

## 4. The Sockeyes Year - Seventeen Games That Changed His Life

The paper trail shows nine games with the Seattle Thunderbirds in 1986-87 (4 goals, 5 assists, 59 PIM in a tiny sample) before something happened that the papers never quite explained - a release, a falling-out, a visa problem, a phone call - and Hervey drove north across the border and turned up in Richmond.

The Sockeyes were the reigning BCJHL regular-season champions. They were coached by Orland Kurtenbach, the former NHL centreman who had captained the expansion Vancouver Canucks and had taken over the Richmond bench that July. Kurtenbach's 1986-87 team would win the Mowat Cup (BCJHL), the Doyle Cup (Pacific region), the Abbott Cup (Western Canadian Junior A), and the Centennial Cup (national Junior A championship) - one of the most complete four-trophy sweeps ever recorded by a Tier II club.

Hervey played only seventeen regular-season games for Richmond. He played all seventeen playoff games that followed. Before he was finished, he had been named to the Centennial Cup All-Star Team.

**Doyle Cup, Game 1, April 7, 1987, Red Deer:** Richmond 11, Red Deer 4. Dave Tomlinson opened the scoring. Just over a minute later, Hervey walked in from the point and made it 2-0. He scored again in the rout. Two goals on a defenceman's night out (Red Deer Advocate, April 8, 1987, image 558334501; Alberni Valley Times, April 8, 1987, image 560107428; Daily Herald-Tribune, April 8, 1987, image 731240705).

**Doyle Cup, Game 2:** Three goals. A hat trick from the blueline. The game-winner came at 11:19 of the third period (Red Deer Advocate, April 9, 1987, image 558334839). There are not many defencemen in Canadian junior history who have scored a Game-2 hat trick in a regional final.

**Doyle Cup, Game 7:** Hervey on the scoresheet again (Alberni Valley Times, April 21, 1987, image 560107877).

**Abbott Cup, Game 4, April 27, 1987, Richmond Arena:** Richmond 5, Humboldt 3. Two Hervey goals (Times Colonist, April 28, 1987, images 508877755 and 508877984; Fort McMurray Today, April 27, 1987, image 738694983).

**Centennial Cup round-robin, May 3 vs Dartmouth:** Richmond 7-3. Hervey again on the scoresheet (Alberni Valley Times, May 4, 1987, image 560108578; The Leader-Post, May 4, 1987, image 496959220).

**Centennial Cup semifinal, May 7 vs Pembroke:** Richmond 9-3. Hervey again (Alberni Valley Times, May 8, 1987, image 560108695; The Leader-Post, May 8, 1987, image 496960544).

By the conclusion of round-robin play he had two goals and three assists and was named to the tournament all-star team on defence (Star-Phoenix, May 9, 1987, image 512097836; The Province, May 10, 1987, image 502033093). Total 1987 Sockeyes playoff line: 17 games, 4 goals, 21 assists, 25 points, 99 penalty minutes - a staggering haul for a defenceman in eleven rounds of tournament hockey. Dave Tomlinson, in later years, would describe his blueline partner as "a heavy sort, with a great powerplay."

The Province noted, almost as an afterthought in its May 11 Centennial Cup wrap, that Hervey had already had a tryout with the Los Angeles Kings (image 502004558). His phone would be busier soon.

## 5. Free Agent - The Winnipeg Jets Camp

Kurtenbach made a phone call. That is the line Hervey himself told the Telegraph-Journal in November 1987, shortly after a New Brunswick tournament in which he had been named to the first all-star team:

> "I'm still on cloud nine from the Winnipeg camp. (Jets' head coach) Dan Maloney was good to me and (general manager) John Ferguson was good to me."
> - Matt Hervey, Telegraph-Journal, November 7, 1987 (image 1111288540)

> "After being cut, Hervey was considering calling it quits but after coming in contact with Orland Kurtenbach... Hervey's outlook on life changed for the better... He helped me make decisions."

Kurtenbach had played for the Jets' brass two decades earlier. His recommendation, once made, carried. Hervey showed up at Winnipeg's main training camp on a pro tryout invitation - undrafted, unsigned, with a resume consisting of two months of Junior A hockey played in the BCJHL. He checked Doug Smail. He fought a Hart Trophy-quality agitator in Boschman. He did not make the Jets. But he did earn the only thing that matters at age twenty-one with no draft pedigree: an AHL contract.

Times-Transcript reported on September 19, 1987 (image 1107104138) that Hervey was in the Jets' rookie-camp lineup for exhibitions against the Manitoba Bisons and noted - crucially for the local audience - that he had "played Tier II under ex-NHLer Orland Kurtenbach."

## 6. Moncton - The Hawks Years

Hervey turned professional with the Moncton Hawks, Winnipeg's AHL affiliate from 1987 to 1992. Over five seasons he played roughly 305 AHL regular-season games for Moncton and recorded more than 1,000 penalty minutes, becoming the club's most durable, most visible defenceman through a period of roster upheaval under three different coaches.

**Year one (1987-88):** On a 21-39-7 Hawks club - last in the AHL's Northern Division - Bowness identified him as the team's "only plus player." On October 28, 1987, in Baltimore, Bowness improvised: he converted Hervey and Jamie Husgen from defence to right wing, and the two of them delivered the hits that sparked a five-goal third-period Hawks rally and a 6-4 win over the Skipjacks. Hervey scored the 3-2 goal at 2:33 of the third (Times-Transcript, October 29, 1987, image 1107148440). A few months later, on January 4, 1988, he opened the scoring on a power-play slapshot 2:13 into a 4-0 Moncton win over Nova Scotia and was named second star (Times-Transcript, January 4, 1988, image 1107105854). He also sat for slashing, unsportsmanlike conduct and a ten-minute misconduct for arguing with referee Mike North during a 5-3 Maine Mariners loss on January 8 (Portland Press Herald, January 9, 1988, image 1309833588).

The fight video archive tells part of the story that the box scores do not. The 1988-89 tape against the Cape Breton Oilers. Another 1989 tilt against Newmarket Saints enforcer Brian Blad. A February 17, 1990 fight with Maine's Rick Boyd. These are not ordinary heavyweight bouts. They are the journeyman AHL defenceman's currency.

**Year two (1988-89):** 73 games, 8 goals, 28 assists, 36 points, **295 penalty minutes** - a career high. Bowness had personally convinced Jets GM John Ferguson to re-sign him (Times-Transcript, April 12, 1988, image 1107141761). Hervey got his first NHL call-up that winter: two games with the Winnipeg Jets, his first taste of the league that had cut him eighteen months earlier. No points, four PIM, but his name was officially in the NHL record book. That February 17, under new coach Alpo Suhonen - who had replaced Bowness when Bowness was promoted to Winnipeg - Hervey scored in an 8-2 rout of Cape Breton that lifted Moncton into a second-place tie (North Bay Nugget, February 18, 1989, image 734711493). The Hawks' AHL playoff run was brief: ten games, three points, 42 penalty minutes, out.

**Year three (1989-90):** Another main-camp audition in Winnipeg. Coach Bob Murdoch, quoted by the Winnipeg Sun on September 28, 1989, delivered the measured scouting report that has followed Hervey through every pro training camp:

> "Hervey is fundamentally sound, but prone to crucial giveaways in his zone."
> - Bob Murdoch, Winnipeg Sun, September 28, 1989 (image 735361916)

Hervey, for his part, knew his assignment:

> "We've got a lot of offensive defencemen here. I'm just looking to provide good defence, clear the front of the net and moving the puck out of our zone consistently."

He scored a pretty preseason goal in a 4-3 Jets win over the Canucks on September 25 (Winnipeg Sun, September 26, 1989, image 735361292) and traded punches with ex-Moncton teammate Brent Hughes in an intrasquad scrimmage earlier that camp (Winnipeg Sun, September 12, 1989, image 735358793). After the Jets claimed veteran Moe Mantha off the NHL waiver draft on October 3, Hervey was sent to Moncton alongside Richison, Bryan Marchment, Bob Essensa and Hughes (Winnipeg Sun, October 4, 1989, image 735358966).

**Years four and five (1990-91 and 1991-92 partial):** 71 games and 47 games respectively, steady plus-minus, the kind of second-pairing AHL defenceman every affiliate depends on and none ever really invites to its NHL Christmas party. By the spring of 1991 he was an unrestricted minor-league free agent.

## 7. Cup of Coffee - The Boston Bruins

In the summer of 1991, the Boston Bruins signed Matt Hervey to a free-agent deal. He would wear jersey number 43.

What followed, in the space of ten days that September and October, is one of the more improbable preseason runs any twenty-four-year-old journeyman has ever put together. His employer was trying to decide whether he could fill the role of seventh defenceman and fourth-line fists on a Stanley Cup-contending roster. Hervey made his case the only way he knew how.

- **September 28, 1991** - Bruins at New York Islanders preseason. Matt Hervey vs Mick Vukota. Vukota was the Islanders' heavyweight. Hervey, in his first Bruins sweater, dropped the gloves.
- **September 29, 1991** - Bruins at St. Louis Blues preseason. Matt Hervey vs Bob Bassen. Back-to-back nights, back-to-back fights.
- **October 7, 1991** - New York Rangers at Boston. Matt Hervey vs Tie Domi. Domi was then the most infamous agitator in the NHL. Hervey, wearing his Bruins sweater in his NHL regular-season debut, fought him to a standstill on Causeway Street.
- **October 9, 1991** - Buffalo at Boston. Matt Hervey vs Brad May. May was a rookie Sabres tough guy who would have a 1,000-game NHL career. Same result.

He had delivered four fights in twelve days against four of the most established heavyweights in the league, and he had done it in his first Bruins training camp and his first four NHL games. He would appear in sixteen regular-season games for Boston in 1991-92 (0 goals, 1 assist, 55 PIM) and five Stanley Cup playoff games (0 points, 6 PIM). He registered one more heavyweight bout on the NHL record:

- **March 23, 1992** - San Jose at Boston. Matt Hervey vs Perry Anderson. Anderson was the Sharks' expansion-year enforcer.

Boston spent most of the regular season moving him between the Bruins and the AHL's Maine Mariners, where he put up 36 games and 47 PIM on loan. It was a cup of coffee, but it was a real cup: he was an NHL defenceman, in an NHL uniform, on a Patrick Division contender, fighting the league's heaviest hands.

That summer - May 1992 - Boston traded Hervey and veteran forward Ken Hodge Jr. to the expansion Tampa Bay Lightning for Darrin Kimble. The Tampa Bay Times would later reference the trade when Kimble delivered a crushing check on Roman Hamrlik in a Bruins-Lightning game on December 12, 1992 (Tampa Bay Times, December 13, 1992, image 324108638).

## 8. Tampa Bay and the Traveling Years

The 1992-93 Lightning were an expansion club in their inaugural NHL season, playing out of the Florida State Fairgrounds' Expo Hall. For a defenceman in Hervey's career bracket, an expansion team is often the best friend he will ever have. He played seventeen games for Tampa Bay - 0 goals, 4 assists, 38 PIM - and registered one more NHL heavyweight fight on tape:

- **March 18, 1993** - Tampa Bay at Toronto. Matt Hervey vs Wendel Clark. Clark was the Maple Leafs captain and one of the most feared hitters in the league. The film survives.

He spent the balance of 1992-93 with the IHL's Atlanta Knights - the Lightning's affiliate and, it turned out, the best hockey team Hervey ever played for. Under coach Gene Ubriaco, the Knights would win the Turner Cup in 1993-94 (Hervey was not on that final roster, having moved on) and were in their inaugural season when he arrived. Ubriaco paired him with veteran Rick Lanz on a first unit that drew notice around the league:

> "Offense from our defense has been a strength of the team. No doubt about it. The defensive pair of Matt Hervey and Rick Lanz may be the most potent offensive-defensemen in the IHL. Hervey has 11 points in 12 games and Lanz has 14 points in 14 games."
> - Gene Ubriaco, Tampa Tribune, November 29, 1992 (image 339097039)

Hervey's 1992-93 Atlanta line: 49 games, 12 goals, 19 assists, 31 points, 122 PIM, plus 9 playoff games. The three-point-a-night defenceman Bowness had first identified in Moncton had, at twenty-six, finally arrived.

Across his three NHL stops - Winnipeg, Boston, Tampa Bay - his totals were 35 games, 0 goals, 5 assists, 5 points, 97 penalty minutes. Including his 5-game Bruins playoff appearance, he remains one of only two 1987 Richmond Sockeyes to reach the NHL for more than a trial (with Dave Tomlinson, who went on to a longer career with Toronto, Florida, Winnipeg and the Islanders).

---

## 9. The IHL Afterlife - Milwaukee, Detroit, Los Angeles

Tampa Bay did not bring him back in 1993-94. He landed with the IHL's Milwaukee Admirals, where he spent two seasons (1993-94 and 1994-95), including a fourteen-game 1995 playoff run, and added four games with the CoHL's Detroit Falcons. His 1995-96 season was spent with the IHL's Los Angeles Ice Dogs - 58 games, 12 points, 81 PIM. After 1995-96 the paper trail goes quiet on Matt Hervey.

---

## 10. Matt Hervey Becomes Matt Block

At some point after his IHL playing career wound down, Matt Hervey began going by Matt Block. The video archive in the 1987 Sockeyes project file - eleven fight clips and a Moncton hockey card - is catalogued in a folder named "Matt Block," reflecting the name under which the clips circulated online in the 2010s and 2020s. Wikipedia's article on the player is filed under "Matt Hervey"; hockeydb, hockey-reference, QuantHockey and the NHL's own historical database all still carry "Hervey." The change appears to have been post-career and informal-to-legal, but contemporaneous reporting on it has not been located in the newspapers.com corpus. None of the 2,350 extracted OCR files in the project archive mention the name "Matt Block" in a hockey context.

The most plausible working theory is a personal name change - a marriage, a reclaimed family surname, or a legal change undertaken for family reasons - sometime between 1996 and the mid-2010s (when the fight videos began appearing under the "Matt Block" label). Until a primary source surfaces, the question remains open. **This is a live gap in the record.**

## 11. Legacy

Matt Hervey played 17 games for the 1986-87 Richmond Sockeyes and registered 25 postseason points from the blueline on the team's four-trophy run. He was named to the Centennial Cup All-Star Team. He made the NHL without a draft pedigree and without a development-league hockey-school resume: he made it by hitchhiking to Langley at fifteen, by taking a recommendation phone call from Orland Kurtenbach, by smacking Doug Smail on his second shift, and by fighting Tie Domi, Brad May, Mick Vukota, Bob Bassen, Perry Anderson and Wendel Clark in an NHL uniform.

Rick Bowness's plain sentence - "He's our only plus player. That says it all." - is the sentence that recurs across every club that employed him. He was, in the vocabulary of his era, a glue guy. Tomlinson called him a "heavy sort, with a great powerplay." Ubriaco called him half of the best offensive blueline pair in the International Hockey League. Bob Murdoch, his most skeptical coach, called him fundamentally sound. None of them disagreed about what they had.

He is one of two Sockeyes from the 1987 Centennial Cup team to reach the NHL in any meaningful role. The other - Dave Tomlinson - is the subject of a separate long-form biography in this archive. The pair of them, on the right side of the Sockeyes' blueline and down the middle of the first line, carried a BCJHL roster across seventeen postseason games and into the only national championship the franchise would ever win.

The California kid from Whittier who showed up in Richmond at twenty, with a Lethbridge brawl on his resume and no particular plan for what came next, was the All-Star defenceman of that tournament. The rest of his career - Moncton, Boston, Tampa Bay, Atlanta, Milwaukee, Los Angeles - is what happened because, for seventeen games in the spring of 1987, he played the best hockey of his life.

In 2025 the British Columbia Hockey Hall of Fame inducted him as part of the 1986-87 Richmond Sockeyes team honour.
