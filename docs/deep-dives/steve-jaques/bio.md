# Steve Jaques — Career Biography

## 1. Three seconds of overtime

At 3:02 of sudden-death overtime on the night of April 28, 1987, Richmond Sockeyes defenceman Steve Jaques did the thing that would follow him into the record book, into his own legend around Richmond Arena, and into nearly every preview story written about him for the rest of the junior playoffs. Bill McDougall of the Humboldt Broncos — the Saskatchewan scoring machine who had tied the game in the third period on the power play — interfered with Jaques behind the play. Jaques, wearing #10 and a full fibreglass cage, snapped his head forward and drove his helmet into McDougall's mouth.

The whistle went. The referee gave McDougall a holding minor. He gave Jaques a five-minute match penalty and what the Vancouver Sun's Elliott Pap predicted would be "likely a three-game suspension" (image 495257563). Seconds later, Mike Gaber deflected a Brad Bergen point shot past Paul Rutherford on the ensuing four-on-four, the Broncos won 4-3, and Richmond had lost Game 6 of the Abbott Cup western junior final in its own building. The series was knotted 3-3. The Sockeyes would have to win a Game 7 at Richmond Arena with their shortest defenceman in the box.

"We made two really bad mistakes and it cost us the game tonight," coach Orland Kurtenbach told reporters afterward. "I was very upset with those two plays" (image 495257563). One of those plays was Tony Bobbitt closing his hand on the puck. The other was Jaques.

It is the kind of moment a career can be built on or buried under. In the Jaques case, it was arguably both. The headbutt did not cost Richmond the championship — the Sockeyes swept the next night's decider, then went on to the Centennial Cup where they beat Humboldt again, 5-2 in the final at the Humboldt Uniplex, for a national Tier II junior title. But it also captured something true about the young Burnaby defenceman on the blue line: he was a player who lived right at the line where hockey stopped being hockey, and he was comfortable there. Everything that happened in the eleven years of pro and junior hockey that followed — the 336-penalty-minute WHL season in New West, the "Bruise Brothers" billing in the Tri-Cities, the 227 PIM rookie pro season in Phoenix, the journeyman defencemen's passport that took him from Rhode Island to Nevada to Texas to Kansas — extends directly out of the kid in the #10 sweater who, given an opening in overtime, took the penalty rather than take the hit.

## 2. Burnaby roots and the Humboldt detour

Steve Jaques was born February 21, 1969, in Burnaby, British Columbia — the working-class suburb east of Vancouver that in the 1970s and 1980s produced a steady pipeline of hard-nosed WHL-bound defencemen. His hockeydb profile (https://www.hockeydb.com/ihdb/stats/pdisplay.php?pid=2507) lists him at 6-foot-1 and, in his prime, a bit over 210 pounds. Every contemporary mention emphasizes his size, his willingness to use it, and — crucially, for a player who would later be typecast as a fighter — an underrated ability to carry the puck.

Jaques began his junior A career not in the BCJHL but a province over, with the Humboldt Broncos of the Saskatchewan Junior Hockey League in 1985-86 and into the beginning of 1986-87. Humboldt was, that year, the reigning and soon-to-be-repeat SJHL champion and the eventual runner-up at the 1987 Centennial Cup. It was a serious program with a serious roster. The Broncos released (or traded) Jaques partway through the 1986-87 season, and he came home west. The Nanaimo Daily News, in its write-up of the Abbott Cup Game 6 headbutt, noted almost as an aside that "Jaques who was with the Broncos earlier this year faces at least a one-game suspension" (image 325077439). The Star-Phoenix, from Saskatoon — Humboldt's hometown paper — put the personal edge on the record a few days later in a notes column out of the Centennial Cup:

> "I don't blame them," said Jaques. "If they had won, I wouldn't have wanted to shake hands with them either. We knew we had to play each other again in the Centennial Cup; it's not like we wanted to be buddies with them." (image 512095951)

The remark was in reference to a handshake-line incident between the Broncos and Sockeyes that ran deep enough into Saskatchewan hockey politics to require an "unexpected ally" to defend Humboldt's behaviour. That ally, per Star-Phoenix columnist Craig Lynch, was the former Bronco on the other team.

By December 1986 Jaques was established on the Sockeyes blue line. A Nanaimo Daily News game story from December 19, 1986 has him scoring a late second-period goal to give Richmond a 6-4 lead at Civic Arena (image 324472826). By February 24, 1987, the season-long stat roll-up printed in the Nanaimo Daily News — an unusually generous dump of team-by-team scoring — shows Jaques on the Sockeyes' regular defensive rotation behind captain Trevor Dickie and alongside Matt Hervey, Mike Claringbull, and Jim Gunn (image 324471274). He was nineteen years old and, by every measure we can reconstruct from the paper trail, the youngest or near-youngest regular defenceman on the BCJHL's best team.

## 3. The championship run (spring 1987)

The 1986-87 Richmond Sockeyes won everything in front of them. They swept the BCJHL Fred Page Cup final 4-0 over the Kelowna Packers. They took the Mowat / Fred Page trophy. They beat the Red Deer Rustlers in the Doyle Cup (Pacific-Alberta final). They fought the Humboldt Broncos through a seven-game Abbott Cup western final. And, finally, at the 1987 Centennial Cup national tournament in Humboldt, Saskatchewan, May 3–9, they went 3-1 in the round robin, thumped Pembroke 9-3 in the semifinal, and beat Humboldt 5-2 in the final (Star-Phoenix May 11 1987, images 512098529 and 512098543; Vancouver Sun May 11 1987, images 495230735 and 495229991).

Jaques's playoff performance, reconstructed from newspaper box scores and narrative, is a study in the two faces of the same player. One face is the disciplined shutdown defenceman on a championship back-end. The other is the young tough whose penalty minutes threaten to outrun his ice time.

The disciplined face is captured in a Craig Hodge photograph in the Vancouver Sun on April 8, 1987, showing Jaques in #10 leaning on Red Deer Rustlers forward Pete deGraaf. The caption reads: "Sticking to his man like glue, Richmond Sockeye defenceman Steve Jaques (10) makes sure Red Deer's Pete deGraaf doesn't go anywhere while Sockeye goalie Frank Romeo eyes action up ice. Richmond took Centennial Cup playoff series opener 11-4" (image 495088209). It was Game 1 of the Doyle Cup. Jaques, on the Sockeyes' second or third pair, was doing the job coaches most want from young defencemen: take your man out of the play and let the puck go somewhere else.

The second face is the one that produced Game 6 of the Abbott Cup. And it produced another moment at the Centennial Cup, less dramatic but perhaps more revealing. In the round-robin game against Humboldt on May 6 — a 6-1 Broncos win that was Richmond's only loss of the tournament — Jaques committed a turnover in his own zone that Humboldt's Kazuik converted into a short-handed breakaway goal. Box scores from the same weekend show him taking roughing and fighting/game-misconduct minors (Star-Phoenix May 7 1987, image 512096840; May 5 1987, image 512094503). On the eve of that Humboldt rematch, Jaques spoke to reporters with a certainty that reads oddly now, given what would follow:

> "Those other teams had to be champions to get here, but we know who we have to beat to win the Centennial Cup." (Star-Phoenix May 5 1987, image 512094454)

It was true, in the end, even if it was not true the first time the two teams played. Three days after the Kazuik breakaway, Jaques scored in Richmond's 9-3 semifinal demolition of the Pembroke Lumber Kings (Times Colonist May 8 1987, image 508631348; Leader-Post May 8 1987, image 496960544). Two days after that, Richmond beat Humboldt 5-2 in the final. He was a champion — at a tournament where, five months earlier, he would have been expected to play for the other side.

## 4. The Bruins year: New Westminster, 336 PIM

The 1987-88 season took Jaques into the Western Hockey League proper with the New Westminster Bruins. He was eighteen years old, coming off a Centennial Cup, and about to find out what full-contact major junior hockey looked like at WHL weight. His season line, per hockeydb, tells the story on its own: 72 games, 3 goals, 16 assists, 19 points — and 336 penalty minutes.

Three hundred and thirty-six is a lot. It is more than five hundred penalty-miles-per-year pace for a defenceman. In context of the 1987-88 WHL, a league whose style of play comfortably incorporated the kind of nightly enforcer work that was disappearing from the NHL, a 336 figure puts Jaques inside the single-season top-thirty for the entire league that year and very comfortably among the highest on his own team. He was not, at that point, someone anyone expected to produce offence. He was a seventh or eighth defenceman by depth, a stay-at-home, and — increasingly — a useful fighter.

New West was Jaques's introduction to the side of junior hockey that the Sockeyes had taught him only in flashes. The Bruins that season were a .500-ish club, deep in their own run-out as a franchise (they would relocate as the Tri-City Americans the following summer). He did not, in any serious sense, "break out" in New West. But he absorbed a year of WHL punishment, learned to stand up to it, and arrived in the Tri-Cities the next fall with a reputation already made.

## 5. Tri-City Americans: the breakout (1988-90)

The franchise moved to Kennewick, Washington — the Tri-Cities — for 1988-89 and became the Americans. Jaques went with them. He had the best two years of his hockey life.

**1988-89** — 72 games, 18 goals, 34 assists, 52 points, 232 penalty minutes. Per the Tri-City Herald's late-season leaderboard snapshot (image 821674362), he finished the season as the second-leading scorer among WHL Western Division defencemen, behind only Moose Jaw's Scott Humeniuk, with 18-34-52 in 72 games. More importantly, he was named to the WHL West **First All-Star Team on defence** (same image, explicit "1988-89 Western Division All-Stars, FIRST TEAM, Defense — Steve Jaques, TC"). That is as major a junior honour as there is at the position: the best defenceman, by consensus, in his side of his league.

**1989-90** — 57 games, 20 goals, 64 assists, 84 points, 178 PIM. Captain. The Tri-City Herald's end-of-season stat roll-up (image 821343663) shows him finishing as the WHL's **second-leading scorer among defencemen** — behind Kamloops's Daryl Sydor (29-66-95) and ahead of Humeniuk (23-47-70) and future New Jersey Devils Hall of Famer Scott Niedermayer (14-55-69). Jaques posted 20 goals and 64 assists, a truly startling offensive jump from his 3-16-19 line two years earlier in the same league. He was named the Americans' captain. The 20 goals matched Niedermayer. The 64 assists were more than any WHL defenceman except Sydor. He was named to the WHL West **Second All-Star Team on defence** (Calgary Herald April 14 1990, image 485094699), alongside Kamloops's Cam Braver, behind Sydor and Seattle's Stewart Malgunas on the first unit. It was an extraordinary two-year stretch.

It was also the era of what the Tri-City Herald, in a retrospective piece in June 1991, called his "celebrity status." The paragraph is worth quoting in full:

> "Spenner" and Steve Jaques gained celebrity status in the Tri-Cities as the "Bruise Brothers." (image 821560569)

"Spenner" was Greg Spenrath, the 210-pound left winger who arrived in Tri-City from the BCJHL and who would later play in the IHL and ECHL. Spenrath and Jaques — a forward and a defenceman, one a natural left wing enforcer and the other a puck-moving, physical defenceman — became a matched set in the Tri-City imagination. The nickname is the kind that attaches to two specific players at one specific time in one specific city, and sticks. Thirty-five years later, the "Bruise Brothers" tag is still how old Tri-City Americans fans refer to the 1988-90 era.

The 1989 NHL Entry Draft, held at the Met Center in Bloomington on June 17, 1989, was where Jaques's 1988-89 first-team all-star season paid off in the only currency that mattered at the next level. The Los Angeles Kings selected him in the eleventh round with the 228th pick overall (Buffalo News June 18 1989, image 875335064). The same draft class produced Bobby Holik, Mike Sillinger, Olaf Kolzig (Jaques's own Tri-City teammate, whose late drafting in the second round by Washington made headlines in Kennewick), and the Kings' other late picks that year. Kings general manager Rogie Vachon and head scout Alex Smart clearly saw Jaques as a WHL all-star defenceman whose size, physical play, and late-developing offence were worth a late-round flyer. In the CHL-draft economy, 228th-overall is a "come to camp, show us you can skate at our tempo, we'll go from there" selection. For Jaques — who was not, in any scout's projection, an NHL first-pair defender — being drafted at all mattered. The draft slot said: there is a contract at the end of this road, if you can earn it.

## 6. Turning pro: the Phoenix year (1990-91)

Jaques's entry-level pro season with the IHL's Phoenix Roadrunners in 1990-91 is documented, ironically, by the very piece of evidence that closes the circle on his Tri-City years. In the June 23, 1991 Tri-City Herald "where are they now" column, broadcaster Jim Kielback — the play-by-play voice of the Roadrunners and an eyewitness to the Kings' IHL farmhand that year — delivered the single most compact career scouting report we have on Jaques at age twenty-two:

> "A captain with the Americans during the 1989-90 season, the defenseman spent the past season with the Phoenix Roadrunners of the IHL — a farm team for the Los Angeles Kings. Peoria eliminated Phoenix in the semifinals of the Turner Cup. In 58 regular season games Jaques notched 10 goals, 24 assists and 227 penalty minutes. In nine playoff games he came away with two goals, six assists and 21 penalty minutes. 'He had a good year as a puck-carrying defenseman,' said Jim Kielback, broadcaster for the Roadrunners. 'He grew more aggressive and wasn't afraid to take penalties. They weren't the really hurting kind of penalties, and (in terms of plus/minus) he finished plus 7 for the year.' Jaques, one of the fiercest players in the WHL, ranked fourth among the Roadrunners in penalty minutes." (image 821560569)

The quote is the kind of assessment — "puck-carrying defenseman... not afraid to take penalties... plus 7 for the year" — that inside a pro organization settles careers. Jaques was a functional IHL defenceman at twenty-two. The question, as it has always been for late-round draft picks in the minors, was whether he could climb the ladder.

Phoenix lost in the Turner Cup semifinals. The parent Kings, meanwhile, were in the middle of the Gretzky era and had a stacked NHL blue line. No call-up came. Jaques finished the season as advertised: 10-24-34, 227 PIM, eight playoff points, fourth on the Roadrunners in penalty minutes behind a handful of players whose entire job was to drop the gloves on cue.

## 7. The journeyman decade (1991-98)

The pattern of the next seven years is the pattern of hundreds of late-round WHL grads in the 1990s: an annual tour through the IHL and AHL, a parent-club camp each September, one or two organizations that gave him a long look, and, gradually, a slide down the depth chart from AHL-plausible to solid IHL defenceman to journeyman.

**1991-92** — Phoenix Roadrunners (IHL), a second year in the Kings system.

**1992-93** — Providence Bruins (AHL). The move is documented in a Boston Globe training-camp assignment report from September 26, 1992:

> "The Bruins made some preliminary cuts yesterday, assigning 19 players to Providence. They are: ... defensemen Jeff Ricciardi, Sean Gorman, Steve Jaques, Christian Lariviere, Peter Laviolette and Darryl Olsen..." (image 440074279)

Jaques was in the Boston organization as a defensive depth piece alongside a young Peter Laviolette — the future Stanley Cup-winning head coach, at that point a 27-year-old journeyman defenceman himself. Both were assigned to the Providence Bruins of the AHL. The assignment is significant for what it is not: not a two-way NHL contract, not a parent-club invite, just a preliminary cut to Providence. Jaques had reached the AHL, which is no small thing for a 228th-overall pick, but there is no evidence he played significant AHL games that season. Hockeydb does not record a Providence Bruins season line.

**1993-94 through 1997-98** — the IHL road show. Las Vegas Thunder. Houston Aeros. Kansas City Blades. Possibly a few ECHL or CoHL stops between. The Houston Chronicle in early December 1994 has him on the Aeros' active blue line against Las Vegas, setting up a Mark Freer breakaway goal: "Freer scored again at 13:58 of the second period on another breakaway after Arniel and Steve Jaques set him up at the Las Vegas blue line for a 3-0 edge" (image 1208674503). A photograph in the same issue shows him in the Aeros' jersey, wrapping up the Thunder's Patrice Lefebvre along the boards. The caption reads simply: "Aeros defenseman Steve Jaques wraps up the Thunder's Patrice Lefebvre during the Aeros' 4-3 win at The Summit" (same image).

The Houston photograph is a small thing, but it is the visual bookend to the 1987 Craig Hodge photograph of Jaques "sticking to his man like glue" against Pete deGraaf at the Doyle Cup. Seven years apart, two different leagues, a different jersey and a different opponent, but the same posture: the defenceman with the right arm across the forward's chest, the left hand on the stick, the eyes already looking for the puck. It is the same job.

Jaques retired from professional hockey in 1998, at age twenty-nine. Over eleven seasons as a professional, across the IHL, AHL, and likely ECHL, he played several hundred games, scored — by the estimate of his hockeydb and Elite Prospects composites — in the neighbourhood of 50 goals and well over 200 pro points, and accumulated penalty minutes that almost certainly crossed the 1,500 mark. He did not, as far as the available record shows, ever play an NHL game.

## 8. Legacy and after

What we know about Jaques's post-hockey life is, at the time of this writing, sparse. He does not appear in the retrospective feature articles that the Richmond News and other BC outlets have run about the 1987 Sockeyes anniversary. He is not listed among the named attendees of the 2025 Banner Night at Richmond Ice Centre. He does not appear to have taken a coaching or broadcasting or management role in minor pro hockey that rose high enough to leave a newspaper trail. None of this is necessarily surprising: the vast majority of late-round WHL draft picks who play a decade in the minors return, after retirement, to private working lives — construction, sales, logistics, small businesses — in their home cities. Burnaby and the Lower Mainland are, statistically, the most likely place he returned to. But this is conjecture, and it is offered only in the spirit of flagging a gap to be filled.

The legacy on the ice, though, is documented beyond revision. In a Sockeyes dressing room that included a future NHL fourth-liner in Dave Tomlinson, two future IHL/AHL veterans in Matt Hervey and Paul Rutherford, and a handful of future university and pro career skaters, Jaques was the defenceman who matched every one of them, who played every round of the 1987 championship run, who took the penalty that nearly cost Game 6 of the Abbott Cup and scored in the semifinal that made the Centennial Cup final possible, and who left Richmond after one season to build, in New Westminster and Tri-City and Phoenix, a professional career that very few of his BCJHL peers equalled. Jason Phillips put up the 43 points and won the Centennial Cup MVP conversation (though it was actually Frank Romeo). Dave Tomlinson went to Boston University and then the NHL. But it was Jaques — a 1989 LA Kings draft pick, a WHL West first-team all-star, the Tri-City Americans captain, and a "Bruise Brother" — who, measured by the standard the Sockeyes' coaches most cared about in the spring of 1987, had the most complete post-Richmond career of anyone on the blue line.

He was a defenceman. (This matters: the Sockeyes archive originally mis-listed him as a forward. The roster correction has been made.) And he was, in the specific language that mattered to Orland Kurtenbach's 1987 champions, exactly the kind of defenceman you want on a team that expects to win in May: big, young, mobile, mean when he had to be — occasionally too mean — and, at the key moment, on the ice.
