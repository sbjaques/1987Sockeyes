Edmonton In the days after Marc Lepine slaughtered 14 female Montreal university students, many Canadian men questioned their own courage. What would they have done if they had been in the classroom when Lepine fired his semi-automatic rifle into the air and ordered the men to leave? Would they have followed everyone else out the door? Or would they have confronted the gunman? One man with no doubts Dave Tomlinson, 56, of Edmonton, president of the National Firearms Association, a small but vocal Canadian gun lobby. Tomlinson says that as soon as Lepine fired, he would have known the e killer, was serious about using the 100-percent, all out attack® using* anything and everything I've got because even if he gets me, the chances are I can knock him out of action long enough so that somebody else can pile on." His decision to attack would have been automatic, Tomlinson says, since he's trained himself to respond in this manner. "If you wait until the day when you're looking at a situation and saying, *My God, in the next few minutes I'm going to be mutilated or killed,' and you try to make your decision to attack then, it's far too late because you temporize and fiddle around until he's had you." One thing in his favor, Tomlinson says, would be that Lepine would get only one, clear shot at him because the Sturm Ruger semi-automatic .223-calibre rifle which Lepine used is extremely difficult to aim after one shot, a fact Tomlinson would have instantly realized from his long years of studying guns.
