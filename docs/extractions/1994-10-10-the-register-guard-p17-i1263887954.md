"It's just something that's taken off elsewhere," said the 52-year-old Crosson, who came on board as manager in February after managing rinks in Southern California for 18 years. "About a year before I left Southern California, a rink opened with an ice rink on one side and a roller rink on the other. Roller hockey really took off." He expects the same thing to happen in Eugene-Springfield. "A lot of kids already have Rollerblades," Crosson said. "You'll see kids on the street, on tennis courts, wherever, trying it." While neither is a particularly forgiving surface if and when you happen to lose your balance and lose the fight with gravity, there are both similarities and differences between ice and roller hockey. "Street hockey is a little slower," Crosson said. "The skating itself is similar. The only difference is the ice gives you a faster game. The contents of a proposal to be presented to the National Hockey League by the players' association today in Toronto will largely determine whether the season will start Saturday or will be postponed again, to Nov. 1 or later. A source on the players' side of the contract dispute said Sunday that calling the plan significantly different from previous versions "would be an excellent way of describing it." In that case, NHL Commissioner Gary Bettman - who has said he would have to see "meaningful" movement before allowing the season to start - might declare enough progress has been made. When he meets with the league's Board of Governors Tuesday in New York, he could accept a no-strike, no-lockout pledge the union made Sept. 29 and get backing for a Saturday start. However, a league source said the new offering won't differ greatly from its last proposal. That plan centered on subsidizing small-market clubs with a fund created by a levy of 5.5 percent on the gate receipts and payrolls of the 16 to clubs. The league rejected that as inadequate. If the plan isn't much different, the governors will reject it and delay the season openers again. Bob Goodenow, executive director of the NHLPA, has been working on his plan since Wednesday, when he rejected two NHL proposals. Those addressed the small-market problem by imposing a 3 percent levy on the gate receipts of the top 16 revenue clubs and a gradual levy on payrolls of a -percent per million dollars up to $14 million and after that, 5 percent for each $325,000. Players said that system would keep salaries low. Two other key issues must also be resolved. It's known the sides agree on most aspects of broadening free agency, but they differ on salary arbitration.
