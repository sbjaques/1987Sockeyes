The Stars stopped Bournemouth Stags 1143 in Heineken First Division play and bowed 9-6 to Cardiff Red Devils in a challenge (exhibition) match in front of 2,500 frenized Wales supporters in other games I caught on my vacation. "I'm having the most fun playing hockey I've ever had," said Yewchin, who joined Oxford 10 games ago after Bournemouth encountered problems paying his weekly salary. "I'd like to play one more year over here before settling down back home," said Yewchin. "The key to a team's success over here is how good your British players are. Most of them are good skaters, but they haven't been taught how to shoot properly."

And the goaltending, is needless to say less than superb. Garry Unger, former NHL ironman, plays for First Division leading Peterborough Pirates and was leading the league with over 200 points when I left last week. Yewchin was nearing the top 10 slot with 120 points. Tony Hand, the Scottish star drafted by Edmonton, was second in Premier Division scoring with 172 points. Al Sims (Boston Bruins) and Steve Moria (exRichmond Sockeye signed by NY Rangers) were also among the Premier top 10 scorers. Oxford, a city with dozens of universities, has had an arena for three years. The Stars attract an average of 500 fans per game, while public skating draws a full house every session. In Cardiff, fans lined up outside 90 minutes before game time. The Red Devils, with star centre John Lawless product) receiving deafening applause everytime he heads up ice in Wayne Gretzky fashion, are a Second Division team expected to move up a division next year. Ken Taggart, vice-chairman of the Stars, has been stationed in Oxford with the U.S. Air Force for 16 years. A Spokane native, Taggart referees a lot of key premier and First Division hockey games. He is always on the lookout for high scoring imports. "An import forward is expected to score between 150 and 200 points and a defenceman 100 points," said Taggart. "And it's easy for a good import to do because you can have lunch in front of the net. The British players don't push the men out from in front." Ex-Finnish First Division player Jari Virta is a big fan favorite in Oxford. The 28-yearold is an awesome stickhandler whose greatest move is one where he flips the puck about eight feet in the air, steps around an opponent and picks up the puck, a move Gretzky wouldn't even try. Heineken has. been a major British Ice Hockey League sponsor for three years and Taggart says the beer company pours 1,200 pounds per club into the First Division (covers cost of referees) and about 3,500 pounds into the Premier Division (covers officials and transportation). OXFORD, ENGLAND Unlike most, Canadian youngsters, British product Paul Simpson wasn't given a new pair of skates moments after he took his first step as a child. "I bought a pair of Bauer Huggers for two pounds," laughs Simpson. "They lasted about six months and then I stole a pair of Micron Mascots from the rink and they lasted 18 months. Then I bought a pair of Junior Tacks for 60 quid and they were dazzlers.' Simpson so of his Junior Tacks that tucked his pants in his skates to show them off at public skating. That was a few years ago though. Today, the 21-year-old comedian and selfprofessed ladies man is a star non-import with Oxford City Stars ice hockey team. He gets five pounds for every point and recently enjoyed a windfall when he collected 10 points in a 27-8 win over Irvine Magnum Wings. He did so playing on a line with imports Don Yewchin (Nanaimo) and Jim Malner (Calgary). In that same game, Yewchin counted 10 goals and six assists and was naturally named Manof-the-Match, an award every fan anxiously waits for at the end of each game. Simpson only started organized hockey five years ago. "I used to play rugby, soccer, and cricket, but I was never any good at any of them," he said. "I saw a hockey match and said (bleep) soccer. I want to play hockey until I die. I want to play hockey and drink beer. I'm only interested in drinking my face off." Simpson admits that his game needs a little work. "I'm going to learn how to skate one day, he jokes. "And my commitment to backcheck is weak. I've got to work on my figure as well." Carl Warnock, a 22-year-old British defenceman who plays it rough, started skating 11 years ago. His first skates weren't anything special either.
