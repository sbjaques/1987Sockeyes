## Paragraphs

There are three parts to every major angling expedition: the preparation and anticipation, the trip itself, and the memories. When the locale is special and everything goes as planned, the trip can be the highlight of an angler's career. But great fishing trips don't just happen; they are carefully planned. And preparation means more than just choosing where to go and buying your plane tickets. It means thinking about the equipment you'll need. This was the case on my recent adventure to the Bristol Bay region of Alaska at the Katmai Lodge. Weeks of preparation preceded my departure. The target fish would be silver (coho) salmon, chrome-bright, fresh from the saltwater and ascending the river on their spawning run. The timing of our visit might coincide with the peak of the migration, which usually begins the last week in July or the first of August and continues through September. We also hoped to catch several other species of Pacific salmon, including chum, sockeye, and pinks (also called humpies). All the salmon respond well to flies or bright spoons and spinners, but I had decided that the fly rod would be my tackle of choice. The recommended fly outfit for silvers and the other salmon mentioned is an 8½- or 9-foot rod built to cast 7- or 8-weight forward lines. Several multiple-piece travel rods by Deerfield and LCI seemed to fit the bill. The reel I picked was a 3M-Scientific Angler System Two, Model 8/9. It is ruggedly built and precision-cast from high silicone-content aluminum alloy. Its adjustable caliper and stainless steel disc drag system have plenty of power to stop the sizzling run of a flashing silver salmon. With a Weight-Forward No. 8 flyline, the reel holds 350 yards of 20-pound test backing. A strongly built reel with a good drag system is a must for Alaskan salmon fishing. For fly lines, I used Scientific Angler Ultras in Weigh Floating models and the new Wet Tip Steelhead design with a 13- foot ultra-fast sinking tip. This line proved to be the one I most used. I had little time to tie flies for the trip. Instead, I packed a kit full of the sparkling bright materials that go into the often gaudy Alaskan fly patterns. I would tie flies on the long plane ride (total over eight hours) and at the Katmai Lodge as well. My Simms neoprene wader system (stocking-foot waders, special felt-soled boots, and neoprene outer wading socks) was the first gear to go into the duffle bag. Katmai Lodge sent out a complete recommendation list of clothing and equipment that was very helpful. Though we did pack lots of insect repellent and head nets, the bugs were troublesome on only one occasion, and only a drop or so of repellent was needed most of the time. Still, this can vary from year to year, and the head net is so light, it should be taken along. I had heard much about the Alagnak River (also called the Branch) and Sarp's Katmai Lodge, located on a high bluff of a bend in the river, about eight miles from tidewater. The Alagnak originates in Nonvianuk Lake in Katmai National Monument. This area lies a few miles south of Lake Iliamna in the heart of the Bristol Bay watershed. The river is part of the designated trophy region and is listed as an official wild and scenic river. Since its origin is not glacial, the Alagnak tends to run clearer than most other Alaskan rivers. Each year, the river welcomes heavy runs of Chinook (King), sockeye, chum, and silver salmon. Every other year, millions of humpies (pink salmon) migrate upriver as well. Huge rainbow trout, Dolly Vardon trout, Grayling, Arctic Char, and northern pike also live in the river and are accessible to anglers at the lodge. Many other Alaskan lodges base their entire operation on daily flyouts. This is not necessary at the Katmai Lodge, since it's on the banks of a prime fishing river. The fishing there is virtually weatherproof, since the best spots are easily reached by the lodge's outboard? powered boats and jet sleds. However, fly-outs are available. and during our stay we made an exciting side trip to Katmai Nai tional Park. So, with our gear carefully assembled and packed and visions of salmon-filled waters in our heads, we boarded our flight in Newark on a Friday morning, and headed toward Anchorage, our first stop in that angler's paradise known as Alaska.
