MORNING BRIEFING In the vault, it's Dial-a-record From Register-Guard and wire service reports It seems only a leg cramp kept Joe Dial from pushing the American pole vault record to a height he said "would have put it out of sight for the American vaulters." Dial broke the American record for the third time in two weeks Friday by clearing 19-4¼ in the Sooner Invitational track and field meet at Norman, Okla. He did it on his first try, then quit after two attempts at 19-7¼ when his leg tightened up. Dial, a former Oklahoma State vaulter and now an assistant coach at Oklahoma, eclipsed the mark of 19-3½ that he set last Sunday in a meet at El Paso, Texas. The highest jump ever by an American is 19- 5½, indoors by Billy Olson earlier this year, but only outdoor efforts are recognized as records. BETTER TIMES ARE COMING ! for fishermen who will be seeking trout and/or kokanee at two high lakes, predict the state fish biologists whose guesses are somewhat educated. They're talking about Wickiup Reservoir and Crescent Lake. For Wickiup, Ted Fies at Bend says he's looking for some real good catches this summer on good-sized browns, rainbows up to five pounds, and coho exceeding 12 inches. His net samples are indicating g good populations and growth for all three species. Coho remaining from the 100,000 stocked last year as fingerlings are averaging 12 inches and are expected to reach 16 later this season. On kokanee, it's good news-bad news. "For the good news," says Fies, "many kokanee survived last year's mysterious die-off. On the bad side, they're small. The maturing age-class, which normally will be running 12 to 14 inches, is averaging 11 inches. "We feel that despite the disease outbreak, Wickiup has an over-population of kokanee." KOKANEE POPULATION in Crescent Lake is coming back gradually after several lean years and fishing this season should be the best in a good many years, ventures John Fortune, fish biologist for this lake and nearby Odell. He bases this largely on increased stocking and apparent good survival in the past two years. The Fish and Wildlife Department had been releasing 100,000 kokanee annually over a long period, to supplement natural production, but boosted that to 150,000 in 1984 and again last year.
