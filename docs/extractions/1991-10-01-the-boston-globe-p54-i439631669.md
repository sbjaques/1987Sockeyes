Sellout or blackout? The Patriots had about 13,000 tickets remaining yesterday for their game in Foxborough Sunday against Miami, making it highly improbable the TV blackout will be lifted. To do so, they must sell out by 1 p.m. Thursday. If the blackout holds, NBC will send Chargers Raiders to Channel 4 at 4 p.m., which will require CBS to feed Channel 7 a 1 p.m. game, Redskins-Bears. But as the secondary Patriots market, Providence's NBC affiliate, Channel 10, will carry two games, preceding Chargers-Raiders with Jets-Browns at 1 p.m., allowing Channel 6, CBS' affiliate in Providence, to carry Cardinals-Giants at 4 p.m. ... Channel 5 will air Oklahoma State-Miami Saturday at noon, leading into the big Syracuse-Florida State telecast at 3:30 ... ESPN will carry a tripleheader, Minnesota-Illinois at 12:30, Clemson-Georgia at 7:30 and Notre Dame-Stanford at 11 p.m., with Sean McDonough and Craig James broadcasting the late-night telecast. No worthy winner The Red Sox Tenth Player Award will be announced on Channel 38 Friday at 7:30, prior to the first pitch against the Brewers. If the station decided to pass on an award this year, there would be no fans picketing the station . ... Thursday night at 7:30, New England Sports Network will bring back the National Hockey League regular season, with the Rangers and Bruins wearing uniforms of the Eddie Shore era to celebrate the league's 75th anniversary. NESN announcers will be unchanged from last season - Fred Cusick on play-by-play with Derek Sanderson and Dave Shea beside him, Tom Larson between-periods host, Steve Burton doing taped features and Shea interviewing between periods. NESN had 358,679 subscribers Sept. 1, down from a peak of 370,000, not bad considering the economic times. Cusick will bring two fine personal numbers to the hockey season, marking his 39th year as a Bruins announcer and observing his 73d birthday Nov. 7 (two years younger than the NHL itself).

It's true enough that the waiver wire isn't about to drop a 50-goal scorer out of the trees, but it could be a place to find, say, a Rick Vaive, an aging wing with a high salary whom the Sabres might bump in favor of younger, fresher talent. For the moment, though, the Bruins have to hope they can wring some much-needed offense out of the low-scoring likes of Wes Walz, Dave Thomlinson, Petri Skriko, Steve Leach, Chris Nilan, Lyndon Byers and Bob Sweeney. None of Boston's forwards looked like feared scoring threats across the exhibition schedule, which the Bruins wrapped up Sunday night in Tampa with a 4-2 loss to the St. Louis Blues. "After a pretty good start in camp, our forwards dropped off and they didn't get better," said Bowness. "We'd hoped that they would keep improving, but they just stopped. Some, maybe this is as good as they'll get. Others, maybe they just need a little more time. I hope so." In net, Blue turned away 17 of 17 shots over half the game Sunday night and appeared to be headed to Boston. However, the Bruins were more impressed early with Matt DelGuidice's work, and it's up to management to decide whether DelGuidice or Reggie Lemelin will dress as Moog's regular reliever. Moog is likely headed to 50-55 starts, particularly with a weak offense in front of him, and an inactive/sporadic backup job could be one better handled by the younger DelGuidice. If so, Lemelin could find himself slowly working into the coaching/consulting job the Bruins defined for him over the summer. "Both [Blue and DelGuidice] proved that they can handle everything thrown at them," said Moog, who relieved Blue Sunday night in Tampa. "That was a show John put on [against the Blues], just spectacular. And Matt, he had a great game in Ottawa that kept us in there. That's what you need." The demotion of Wiemer left the core of Ray Bourque, Garry Galley and Glen Wesley intact, with Glen Featherstone an obvious member now of the Front Four.
