Events of the last few days have placed the Oilers under intense pressure. Much is self-imposed. They remain the most talented team in the National Hockey League. Man for man, none of Edmonton's rivals is close. But hockey isn't played mano a mano. It was designed as a team event. At their best, the Oilers understand the concept. They handle the puck unselfishly. They cover up for each other. Win or lose, they spread the praise and share the blame. At their worst, it's a different story. Outsiders are left to search for any real semblance of closeness. A lot of media guys guess that it hasn't. The impression of conflict goes back to last spring, when coaches kept insisting the Oilers refused to follow instructions in the disaster against Calgary. Things don't seem much better this time around. Boss Glen Sather maintains the Oilers are lovers not fighters - although he's convinced they can look after themselves. First he talks about the need for increased team discipline. Then he wonders whether there's any point in counting on officials to call even the most blatant of penalties against Oiler opponents. He should talk more, I submit, about the dismal checking that surfaces sometimes - as it did when Rich Chernomaz punched home a loose puck for an important New Jersey goal in the third period after Andy Moog had turned in a spectacular save. He should talk about the number of times Oiler players have been left free to avoid interviews - especially after they play poorly. He should talk about the fact that the Oilers have a choice. They can follow instructions, work hard and give themselves a chance to win their third Stanley Cup in four years and go down in history as an outstanding team. Or they can pout when things get tough, blame events beyond their control and label themselves for all time as underachievers.

(AP) Doug Gilmour had one goal and three assists and Bernie Federko had a pair of goals and one assist as the St. Louis Blues, behind four power-play goals, trounced the Minnesota North Stars 8-5 National Hockey League victory Monday night. Gilmour's 34th goal of the season came during a three-goal spurt in a 3:42 span midway through the opening period to give the Blues a 3-2 lead. Minnesota had enjoyed a brief 2-0 lead on goals by Keith Acton and Brian MacLellan. The Blues scored at 11:06 when Mark Hunter put in his 35th goal of the season on NHL the power play. Gilmour tied the game 2-2 just 28 seconds later when he grabbed Tim Bothwell's wide shot behind the net and circled in front to beat North Stars goalie Don Beaupre. The Blues went ahead 3-2 at 14:48 on Federko's first goal on a rebound shot off the boards by Greg Paslawski. St. Louis got another power-play goal at 9:15 of the second period to take a 4-2 lead. Mark Reeds got the goal after taking a pass from Federko on a 2-on-1 break. The North Stars pulled within 4-3 at 12:29 of the second period on Dirk Graham's goal. But the Blues got four goals in the third period, including Federko's second on the power play. St. Louis's other goals came from Paslawski and Ron Flockhart, and an open-net goal by Rick Meagher. The North Stars got third -period goals from Bob Rouse and Mark Pavelich.
