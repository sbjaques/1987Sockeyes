## Paragraphs

Archaeologists are excited about the finds made at the Anglo-Saxon site near Flixborough. Dig supervisor Dave Tomlinson studies one of the items, a bronze square-headed brooch.

the site were incredible - more than 400 pins and 600 loom weights with everything dating to the Middle Saxon period. Around eight or nine archaeologists under the supervision of Dave Tomlinson and Humberside's principal archaeologist Ben Whitwell have been working on the site and they have discovered post holes and wall trenches which indicate the position of 14 timbered buildings including two which had been rebuilt several times during the 200 year life of the settlement. Perhaps one of the most unusual finds was a small lead plaque inscribed with seven AngloSaxon names, several of which might be associated with the religious establishment of the 7th to 8th century Kingdom of Lindsey. Aid to memory Mr. Leahy added that a gilt bronze ring, inscribed with the first half of the alphabet, could have been used as an aid to memory or prayer was also found nearby. An impressive collection of writing implements has also been found with an elegant silver stylus - possibly unique - giving evidence of the high status of its owner. Middle Saxon material has been found at other locations in the area and the Flixborough settlement probably fitted into a network of monastic or aristocratic settlements within the Kingdom of Lindsey. Some of the finds and other dislays associated with the dig have just been displayed at a special preview at Normanby Hall where all those associated with the dig were able to view the finds as well as take the opportunity of visiting the site itself.
