Narver, now a Canadian citizen, enjoyed being on the ground floor for the construetion of new spawning channels on the Babine. He is proudest, however, of his work on steelhead enhancement. When he joined the provincial Fish and Wildlife e Department, the graphs on steelhead fishing were dismal. Angling days, the numbers of steelhead retained and total catches all were declining in B.C. It Narver who helped introduce catch-release fishing on steelhead rivers. Only hatchery-reared fish can be kept on Vancouver Island. The province's program has had dramatic results. Fish retention has improved from the 1981 low of about 10,000 to 20,000 in just five years. Angling days hit a low of 120,000 in 1981 and went to 220,000 by 1985. The greatest increase has been in total catch. From a low of 40,000 in 1978 and '79, catch totals (catch-and-release figures for the province) have skyrocketed to 150,000 by 1985. The results could mean steelheaders may soon be allowed to retain some wild stock. "We may have to restrict or two rivers, "We're looking at the possiblity," he confided. and it will probably be done on a tag system." He warns, however, that there is still much work to be done before all steelhead stocks can be declared safe and healthy again. Narver has a way of achieving success. His research on the Chignik River - a system with two major lakes and "homes" for 12 different stocks of sockeye - has resulted in one of the most successful salmon fishery in Alaska. "On a per capita basis, fishermen along the river are the most succesful in the world. Most of them are natives." Narver's early research produced optimum guides for containing limits that are still followed by the state today. There is no denying that Narver has great "inside" respect, the kind that comes from fellow biologists who know and understand technical finery. But there is greater impact when respect is voiced by people "in the field." They i can be enforcment officers, environmentalists or just ordinary game club members. They say David Narver is doing a good job.

"I'm not sure I would do that again," he laughed, "especially since discovering how badly bears can smell." His encounters with Alaska's brown bears - the largest in the world - came while he was researching sockeye runs in Alaska, including the Chignik River. During one 13-mile walk along the river, Narver and his partner counted 40 of the magnificent beasts. "We saw that many because he made the walk with the wind always against us," he explained. "On the way out, with the wind behind us, we never saw one." To those bears, he said, man was a complete stranger and Narver often was able to pass safely - while only 20 or 30 feet away. That was in the 1950s, before the advent of fly-in fishing and hunting trips. "They also were pretty well fed. I think every bear in Alaska used to head down to those rivers for the spawning runs of the salmon. They gorged themselves on salmon and weren't interested in anything undelectable as a biologist." He is 52 today. Dave and wife Barbara are the parents of four boys. "If I can get it right," Narver says he is able to introduce sons Doug, 28; Jeff, 26, Stephen, 25, and Chris, 16. Chris has his father's hotshot enthusiasm for fishing and hunting. He bagged his first deer when he was 11 and has shot five bucks in the last four years. A 1956 graduate from Oregon State, Narver worked in three years as a navigation instructor with the U.S. Air Force before returning to Washington to get his masters degree.
