But '86 represents the start of chapter two in the Leah Pells story. She re-enrolled at SFU in the fall and her comeback trail began rather modestly in November, when she placed fifth in the Canadian CrossCountry championships. Then, in the spring of '87, she ran off a series of impressive performances, highlighted by her NAIA-record time of four minutes, 18 seconds in the 1,500. Pells also came under the wing of SFU assistant track coach Mike Lonergan in '86-87. Lonergan recognized her great potential - and also the fact that she needed "a good oldfashioned kick in the butt" once in a while to realize it. "Leah had lost all her confidence as a runner after the 1983 season," recalls Lonergan. "She was outstanding as a junior, but then she would have to move up to senior competition at age 19. I think she was intimidated by that ... all of a sudden she realized she'd be up against people like Lynn Williams, Brit McRoberts, Debbie Bowker. ... in short, the best (women) middle distance runners in Canada." Pells has since learned to be confident in her own ability and not to become discouraged when she runs a poor race. "It used to be that you wouldn't see Leah for two weeks after she had a bad race," says Lonergan. "Now she's back on the track the next day working even harder." Pells' new attitude is reflected in her performances over the last two years: An undefeated cross-country season in '87 that included six NAIA District One course records; a NAIA national cross-country record, and a fourth-place finish at the world cross-country trials in February that qualified her for the world championships March 26 in Auckland, N.Z. These and other performances put Pells in a February issue of Sports Illustrated, where she was pictured in the Faces in the Crowd feature. Pells says that qualifying for the worlds in Auckland is the biggest accomplishment of her career so far. But her ultimate goal is to represent Canada in the Olympic Games, either this year in Seoul or in 1992 in Barcelona. The 1,500 is Pells' best chance for a ticket to the Olympics. Her career best in the race is 4:17 and the minimum Olympic standard set by the Canadian Olympic Association is 4:07. Pells will be trying to win an Olympics berth by finishing in the top two at the Canadian Olympic trials next August in Ottawa. "I'm just 23, so I won't be terribly disappointed if I don't make it to Seoul," says Pells. "I can always try again for 1992, when I should be at the peak of my ability.
