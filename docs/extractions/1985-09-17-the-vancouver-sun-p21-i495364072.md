Meantime, Butler's 48-year-old father, his voice straining with emotion, urged compulsory protectors for all players. "I couldn't possibly imagine another parent going through torture I'm going through," he said. "It may seem like over-protection, and it would have seemed like overprotection to me as well but, when the tragedy happens, it's unbearable. "A neck protector would have saved my son's life and it would have been nice to have it be mandatory. I wouldn't have said this before this happened." "There's a limit to how much protection you can put on but, once it happens to someone you love, it changes it all around. "I couldn't have loved any person more than Jeff. He was the greatest son anyone could ask for.' Butler, who played in a commercial hockey league in Regina when he was younger, said he isn't bitter that his

son, a Grade 12 student at Semiahmoo senior secondary, was not required to wear a neck protector. "*Canadian amateur hockey and all hockey associations, they've done so very much to help protect the boys from being injured with all the hockey equipment and all the padding and everything they have out now. *And to make them 100-per-cent protected is almost an impossibility. So there is certainly no blame." But the self-employed businessman said he believes hockey players will wear special safety equipment only if they are forced to because they don't associate equipment used infrequently with being part of the game. He said his son, who played hockey since he was five, dedicated his life to the sport. "He lived it, he dreamt it and after every game he'd come home wake me up and give me a hug and tell me all about his hockey night. About the good things and the bad things. He just lived it . . ." However, Henry Reimer, 18, victim of a similar accident last year, said he will not wear a protector. "That is a 1 freak accident," Henry Reimer said of his injury last November and the one that killed Butler. It took 50 stitches to close Reimer's cut. "I was out a month and a half," Reimer said from Chicago where he is on a hockey scholarship at the University of Illinois. "I wore one of those football (braces) for two weeks when I came back." Reimer said he "got a chill" when he heard about Butler. But he said he would not wear a protector because he does not think such an accident is likely to happen to him again. Reimer, 18, is a Matthew McNair high school graduate and was playing for Richmond Sockeyes when he was cut. "I saw it coming but couldn't react." He said he became confused and had no idea how serious his injury was. "If it was millimetres either way, he would be a statistic," said Vic LeMire, Reimer's coach last season.
