## Paragraphs

Andrew Hawker and Denis Claringbull surf around the moral issues surrounding the digital revolution and the need for regulation

However, anyone with a modestly powered PC and some suitable software, can offer a service on the Internet. Add a certain amount of ingenuity in the use of forwarding addresses and the operator can become virtually untraceable. As readers consider these issues (no doubt mainly through the old-fashioned medium of newsprint stuffed through the letterbox) it may be tempting to assume that the cyber-punks and the net-surfers are merely getting their comeuppance. It is fair enough to argue that, since most subscribers have volunteered to join the Internet, they have only themselves to blame if they find it a bit anarchic. However, technology has a habit of moving from the optional to the essential. The Internet is still, relatively speaking, comparable to the early days of radio when people strained to hear what was coming through on their -made sets. Nowadays we regard radio as indispensable, if only to allow us to hear the traffic news o on BRMB or to make calls on portable phones. Once the cables start to hum with new interactive service, businesses may find they need to take part to compete. Subscribers may then find they have to pay a premium price if they want to stay with more traditional methods of trading. When this happens, it will be important that everyone can turn to the new networked services with a high degree of trust. Van we depend on self by the service providers? Would an "information services police" be a threat to freedom of expression? Will companies selling goods to people in their homes be able to gain much too detailed a picture of their tastes and preferences? Will it ever be possible to prevent countries from providing safe havens for the "information pirates?" In the Birmingham Centre for Business Ethics, we regard this as something which should be debated well in advance of the day when the doors open on the new electronic marketplace. Since Birmingham is well placed to exploit the new communications technology, it would be good to see us leading the way in both the business and d the ethics. Andrew Hawker is a Lecturer in Information Technology, Department of Accounting in Finance, University of Birmingham. Denis Claringbull is Senior Industrial Chaplain, Birmingham Diocese. The authors are founder members of the Birmingham Centre for Business Ethics.
