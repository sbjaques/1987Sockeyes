There is, he implies, a very delicate line to be drawn, a tightrope to be walked, and a great deal of stress, distress, and sheer bad feeling to be dissipated. You wonder how he copes. Co-counselling is a unique form of off-loading problems in which two people trained and chosen for their sympathy and compatibility alternately act as counsellor and client. "We tend to meet at fairly regular intervals, sit on the floor, hold hands and simply let the problems rip," he says. "For the session one of us is counsellor, the other the counselled." At the end of the session the "client" is "wiped clean" of his - or her problems. On the other hand the counsellor is usually "wiped out". Taking on someone else's problems at this heightened level is exhausting work. In the New Testament Jesus Christ described the energy drain of performing miracles as "Virtue is gone out of me." Canon Claringbull knows exactly what he meant. But, he says, and this is the priest speaking, the pool of Christian love is inexhaustible; faith often does move mountains, and his faith in a benevolent Father is unshakeable. Old-fashioned perhaps he still uses the great King James Bible and the Book of Common Prayer for services at St Paul's but his faith is as strong as the steel core of his newly-built church spire, and as far-reaching as the power of prayer itself.
