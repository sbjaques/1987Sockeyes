She did admit that the pulp and paper industry is not attracting many women because of the dirt. But, she said, the dirt did not bother her. She said 25 percent of the engineers in her area are women, adding that there were no women in the Tech section except for one co-op student. MacBride also said she wanted to stay in Maine, so she decided to get into pulp and paper. Scott MacBride, a maintenance engineer, said when he was in college, he had no idea of what he wanted to do. He said that when he came to Boise he did not know what an engineer did. He said he looked at a lot of things and solved the problems or improved them. Hand drafting is not used in engineering as much as it use to be. Computerized drafting is faster. He said in his job he relies mostly on practical knowledge and has not used much of his physics and calculus. The machines now are more computerized, he said, so a person can control the process. He, too, expressed the importance of English in engineering. Steve Czenczek, production engineer, said he grew up in a small mill town like Rumford. He, like Mrs. MacBride, got interested in pulp and paper' in his junior year in high school. The pulp and paper industry had a 100 percent employment rate out of school. He said the jobs he had were in process, environmental and energy engineering. He told of working with Richard Burns, project manager on a pipe project which saved Boise 1.2 million dollars.
