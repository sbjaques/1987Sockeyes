Mariucci's "godfather" tag was a tribute to the many great hockey minds he fostered. The beleaguered Edmonton. Oilers did their absolute best Monday night to give the appearance of a team heading for disaster. Before the game, the Oilers ran the risk of thinking their appearance against the New Jersey Devils might prove something. Whatever Edmonton wanted to think - whatever the players think now - they proved nothing positive. They can't be blamed for seeking a crutch, I suppose, but any player who claims to notice a similarity between the Devils and the Calgary Flames is fooling himself. Sure, the Oilers still have a bit of a cushion in their ongoing bid to stay ahead of the Philadelphia Flyers and the hated Calgary Flames for first place overall. Staying there will mean extra money, but beating the weakest team in the Patrick Division isn't calculated to build confidence something the Oilers sorely need since their self-esteem was shaken by losses to Calgary in back-to-back weekend games. Besides, finishing first in the regular season is feeble consolation for the prospect of taking an early power from the Stanley Cup playoffs for the second year in a row. Events of the last few days have placed the Oilers under intense pressure. Much is self-imposed. They remain the most taiented team in the National Hockey League. Man for man, none of Edmonton's rivals is close. But hockey isn't played mano a mano. It was designed as a team event. At their best, the Oilers understand the concept. They handle the puck unselfishly. They cover up for each other. Win or lose, they spread the praise and share the blame. At their worst, it's a different story. Outsiders are left to search for any real semblance of closeness. A lot of media guys guess that it hasn't. The impression of conflict goes back to last spring, when coaches k kept insisting the Oilers refused to follow instructions in the disaster against Calgary. Things don't seem much better this time around. Boss Glen Sather maintains the Oilers are lovers not fighters although he's convinced they can look after themselves. First he talks about the need for increased team discipline. Then he wonders whether there's any point in counting on officials to call even the most blatant of penalties against Oiler opponents. He should talk more, I submit, about the dismal checking that surfaces sometimes as it did when Rich Chernomaz punched home a loose puck for an important New Jersey goal in the third period after Andy Moog had turned in a spectacular save. He should talk about the number of times Oiler players have been left free to avoid interviews - especially after they play poorly. He should talk about the fact that the Oilers have a choice. They can follow instructions, work hard and give themselves a* chance to win their third Stan-: ley Cup in four years and go down in history as an outstanding team.
