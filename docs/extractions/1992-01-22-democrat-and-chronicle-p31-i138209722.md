## Paragraphs

When Matt Hervey's name appears in the newspaper, it is always on the sports page in a hockey story. It has been that way for years now, beginning with his junior career in western Canada and continuing into his professional career in the American Hockey League. A rugged fifth-year defensemen for the Maine Mariners, Hervey is a great example of determination rewarded, how an unheralded, mildly-gifted player from East Los Angeles can work his way into a chance at the National Hockey League. But had hockey not been available to him as a teen-ager in crime-ridden East L.A., it's possible that his name would have by now appeared in a news story regarding a stabbing, drive-by drug shooting, or other gang-related violence on an L.A. street. At around age 14, he was carrying a knife and jockeying between gang and jock peer groups in school. "I wasn't real bad, I kept a lid on myself," said the 25- -old Hervey, who will miss tonight's 7:35 game against the Rochester Americans at the War Memorial because of an injury. "I was getting in gang fights but I never pulled a knife or stuck anyone. You have to understand, you're the victim or someone else is." He definitely didn't want to be the victim so he steered away. Then hockey allowed him a clean escape when he was 16. Having played organized hockey since age 5, he made several youth a teams from Whittier, Calif. That provided exposure, and junior scouts gave him the opportunity to play at the Tier II level in Langley, British Columbia, then major junior in Victoria, British Columbia, and Lethbridge, Alberta. "I haven't been back in nine years," he said. "I don't ever go there. I haven't even seen the house where I grew up. There's too many crazy people in the big cities." The fence surrounding his high school in Lamirada was topped with barbed wire. "For security," he said. That didn't deter after gang fights, which became events. "Half the school knew about them. It'd be like a concert crowd. "On the weekends, you'd cruise around and look for a fight with Mexicans, or usually they looked for one with you." Basically, he was a victim of the area and circumstances. "I didn't have the guidance I think I needed," he said. That's because his parents separated when he was 14. His father left while his mother "married a real idiot." He has seen his father only once or twice since leaving for Canada to pursue a hockey career. Two years ago his mother was involved in a car accident. She has been in a coma ever since. He doesn't ask for pity. "It hasn't been a rosy upbringing; I've seen a lot better and I'm sure others have gone through worse. That adversity either makes you stronger as a person or it gets the best of you." He has become a strong person. After signing with the Winnipeg Jets organization as a free agent in 1987, he spent his first four seasons with the AHL's Moncton Hawks. He played two games in Winnipeg. This season, he joined the Boston Bruins organization and has played four games in Boston under his former coach at Moncton, Rick Bowness. "It's tough to make it on your own," he said, "but if you want something bad enough, it's there for the taking.'
