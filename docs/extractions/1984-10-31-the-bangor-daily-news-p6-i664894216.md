Bangor Daily News On the Ice By Larry Mahoney Defense is considered by some to be the most difficult position to play in hockey, because its cerebral responsibilities are just as demanding as the physical aspects. But three University of Maine-Orono freshman defensemen, Stan Czenczek, Dave Nonis and Scott Drevitch, have made a rapid adjustment to the Division I blue line. And they are three of the' reasons the Bears had allowed just 15 goals (including two empty-netters) in their first four games before Minnesota erupted for eight on Saturday night. That's an average of just 3.25 per game (excluding_ the empty-netters). "You don't find too many defensemen who can step in and help out immediately at this level," said senior defenseman Rene Comeault. "That says a lot about their ability. They've learned a lot and have adapted unbelievably well to the defensive aspect of the position." "I've been really impressed with them," agreed junior defenseman Scott Smith. "Stan is one of the most physical players on the team while Dave and Scott are really good with the puck." "I thought Stan and Dave could step in and play well immediately but I didn't expect them to play as well as they have,' said Coach Shawn Walsh. "And Scott has been a pleasant surprise. haven't seen three freshman defensemen do such a good job in a long time." Walsh said that in addition to their physical skills, they are confident individuals. And those attributes have enabled them to become such valuable performers so early in their careers. "They aren't freshmen, mentally," said Walsh. The adjustment is a difficult one but Walsh has made the adjustment much easier according to the trio. "Coach Walsh deserves the credit," said Nonis. "I've learned more in a month and a half here than I had learned in my previous 12 years." All of his drills are gameoriented and that has helped out a lot," said Czenczek, who admitted that he figured "the adjustment would be a lot tougher than it has been." Czenczek said the pace is much quicker than the one he is used to. "There's a lot more pressure on you so you have to think a lot faster and move the puck a lot more quickly," said Czenczek. "You don't have much reaction time so you've got to know where to pass the puck ahead of time," said Drevitch. The three of them are used to being offensive-minded defensemen because that is what was stressed in their previous leagues. Drevitch, an Andover, Mass., native, played for Northwood Prep School (N.Y.) while Czenczek and Nonis played Western Canadian Tier II hockey in British Columbia last year. "Our team was so good that defense wasn't stressed," said Drevitch. "I'm learning : a lot about it." "I was told to take the puck and skate with it last year,' said Nonis, who played for his hometown team, Burnaby. "So I've still got to improve my team game. I've got to pass the puck more and get better at passing it." Nonis, like the other two, said he feels he is a much better defensemen now than he was at the start of training camp because of the information he has absorbed about the position, particularly the defensive aspect of it. "I've learned a lot about the little things like positioning and who to pick up in certain situations," said Nonis. The three of them recognize the importance of the defensive aspect of their games. Playing an offensive brand of hockey may be fun, but winning is more enjoyable and, as Nonis said, "without good defense, you aren't going to win many games." "If you want to go somewhere (with hockey) after college, you've got to learn to play defense," added Czenczek.
