FARGO (AP) Mike McCormick has willingly accepted the risks for the way he wrestles. But he wasn't so willing to accept the hazards for the way he partied. After nearly four years marred with an arrest for possession of marijuana, probation, drug testing and rehabilitation, McCormick finally feels upbeat about his life and wrestling career at North Dakota State. The junior from Bismarck is favored to win the 177-pound championship at today's North Central Conference meet in Sioux Falls, S.D. His risky style has produced a team-high 11 pins. But the risks he has taken on the mat seem insignificant compared to the ones he took while he partied during high school. "I was raising a little bit of hell," McCormick said. "I was boozing it up and smoking dope.' He was arrested during his senior year for possession of a controlled substance with the intent to sell on school property. MINOT - In the five years Minot hockey official Thor Nelson has worked the sport, he has never had a player come up to him and ask him his name. That changed Thursday night when he made his National Hockey League debut before a crowd of 21,- 000 people at the United Center, Chicago's new stadium for the Blackhawks. A player skated up to him right away and asked him what his name was. They knew he was the new stripes in town. "They want to know who you are," Nelson said. Nelson is a linesman. They call offside, icing, work faceoffs and break up altercations. He didn't need to break any up Thursday night when Chicago and Calgary skated to a 2-2 overtime tie. He was scheduled to work another NHL game Friday at Detroit. "I'm very happy with my game,' Nelson said late Thursday night. "During the first period, I had a big old knot in my stomach. There was only one scuffle, no fights. There will be plenty of those in the future.' He defines a scuffle as pushing and shoving, whereas a fight in the NHL is when it comes to blows. "It was real fun. I'm glad it's over," Nelson said. His supervisor was at the game, but he didn't go over the game with him. "No news is good news," Nelson said. Nelson is in his fifth year of reffing. This is his first year of working the NHL, the American Hockey League and the International Hockey League. He spent two years in college and junior, in addition to four years of high school and minor league hockey. "This is a lot faster," Nelson said. "The guys are bigger, the plays are so crisp. There is SO much going on. There are players making millions of dollars and there's no time to think about it. For 3 hours, you go hard." Prior to the start the season, he runs 3 to 5 miles a day, rollerblades and does a lot of biking. "Skating is like biking; you never forget," Nelson said. The call to the bigs came after he worked the gold medal game at the 1994 U.S. Olympic Festival. Until now, those were the best skaters Nelson had seen. After the Festival, he attended an NHL camp in Hamilton, Ontario, in September. He will put on 100,000 miles in the air this year. How many miles he puts on the ice is yet to be determined.
