VANCOUVER - Bob Nystrom plans court action against the National Hockey League Players' Association to champion the cause of the borderline player. Recent changes in the NHLPA's disability insurance plan and the new collective bargaining agreement with the league have cost the New York Islanders' assistant coach a potential $500,000 in lost benefits. He said this week he isn't out for himself or is trying a squeeze play on the union, but wants to show that all NHLPA members are not equal when it comes to claiming a disability benefit. "I'm not bitter, I'm not crying the blues and I'm not trying to drain the last bit of money out of the NHLPA," said the 34-year-old former right winger who was forced to retire from the Islanders last season because of an injured left eye. Nystrom last played for the Islanders under a termination contract the lowest form of NHL job security which he signed in August, 1985. A month later an insurance company threatened to cancel the disability insurance plan unless the NHLPA drastically revised its payment schedule. Under the new disability agreement, all players under termination contracts, mostly older and fringe players, are excluded from coverage. Nystrom was denied a claim. Previously, any player who suffered a career-ending injury, regardless of age or contract status, would be compensated for $175,000 (U.S.). "I looked into it and found about 13 per cent of all NHL players are playing on a termination," Nystrom said. "These guys are still members of the NHLPA, they pay their dues, they're still playing the same tough, hard game and they're open to the same danger of being injured as anyone else, yet they're not covered." Alan Eagleson, executive director of the NHL.PA, said players on termination contracts became ineligible for disability benefits after Sept. 15. 1985, because too many players took advantage of the system.

Kelowna goaltender Dusty Imoo and a powerful defence helped the Packers trip the Juan de Fuca Whalers 5-2 in B.C. Junior Hockey League action Thursday at Juan de Fuca Recreation Centre. The Whalers outshot the Packers, who hold down first place overall in the league with a 40-3-2 won-lost-tied record, 33-27. But the home club still trailed by period scores of 3-1 and 4-1 before a crowd of 680 fans. Bruce Bianchini, Pat Mangold, Rob Steele, Kerry Russell and Mike Murinko scored for Kelowna. Phil Soukoroff and Sheldon Nohr replied for Juan de Fuca, which has a 16-30 won-lost record. The third period proved chippy with three major fights breaking out. Murinko, Stan Marple and Kevin Tallifer of the Packers and Ron Bhala, Dino Buttizoni and Bruce Courtnall of the Whalers each received five-minute majors for fighting and game misconducts during the final 10 minutes of play. Kelowna's strong defence repeatedly kept the Whalers at bay. Juan de Fuca players who did get through were stopped short by Imoo, although three of the Juan de Fuca shots hit a post. The Whalers face the Richmond Sockeyes at 8 p.m. Saturday in Juan de Fuca.

Times-Colonist news services Keith Crowder scored from close range at 19:07 of the third period and the Boston Bruins edged struggling Pittsburgh 6-5 in a National Hockey League game Thursday night despite three goals and two assists by the Penguins' Mario Lemieux. The game at Boston ended in a bench-clearing brawl as Pittsburgh's Randy Cunneyworth and Crowder traded punches after the buzzer. There were a few side battles before a slugfest between Dan Frawley of the Penguins and Jay Miller of the Bruins touched off a general melee that lasted for several minutes. Crowder poked in his 13th goal just before he tripped over teammate Charlie Simmer, who pushed the puck away from goalie Gilles Meloche while stretched out on the ice.
