Kenai Peninsula Despite Megan's monster lingcod, most anglers continue to target salmon in Seward, where $5,000 awaits the lucky angler who catches the heaviest silver. Fishing has been above average, mainly because the silver, salmon run is at least a week than normal. Fishermen averaged one silver every five or six hours during the first three days of the derby. On Wednesday, state biologists had checked 60 sport-fishing boats as of 6:30 p.m.; the total catch on those boats p. was 200 silvers. Shoreline fishermen weren't doing nearly as well. The preferred method seems to be trolling, using either downriggers or diving planars with herring. On the other side of the peninsula, red salmon are turning red, so Kenai River fishermen are shifting their attention to silver salmon. Dave Nelson, area management biologist with the Department of Fish and Game, the Kenai's run of sockeye or red salmon "is just about over. Most of the sockeyes have assumed their spawning colors and won't be attractive to fishermen." The same is true for the Russian River, which will be closed to the taking of red salmon Aug. 21. While the red run is nearly ended, the Kenai's run of silvers is "improving daily," Nelson says. The large majority of bright silvery coho are being taken below the Soldotna bridge. Most anglers fishing the Kenai River for silver salmon anchor up and bounce egg clusters off the bottom or use artificial lures such as Pixees. Rainbows and Dolly Varden are also available throughout the Kenai River. Silver salmon have also begun entering several other popular peninsula streams, including the Kasilof, Anchor and Ninilchik rivers and Deep Creek. Fishing has been fair at best on those streams. Fishing for Dolly Varden on lower peninsula streams is rated fair. Nelson reminds anglers who fish at the Kasilof River/Crooked Creek confluence to "respect private property." An enhanced of silvers is under way at Six-Mile Creek, which is accessible from the Hope Highway. Fishing remains good to excellent for halibut in Kachemak Bay. Shoreline fishermen are also beginning to take silvers at Mud Bay, which is located next to the Homer Spit. Mud Bay is a high-tide fishery; Pixees are reported to be the preferred lure.
