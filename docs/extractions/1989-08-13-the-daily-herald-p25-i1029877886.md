TACKLE: Standard trolling setup is a flame orange crescent mooching sinker of 6 8 ounces (assuming you're not using downriggers), five feet up the line from a white size "0" dodger. The dodger or flasher is followed by a short leader, two or two and one times the length of the dodger or flasher (but not much longer than 18 inches). Tie on a size 3/0 barbless hook, slide a red plastic bead down on top of the hook, and a fluorescent pink mini- squid (hoochie) on top of that. Spoons like the Martin Red Magic or the red-head FST, or lures like the fluorescent red Guppy in place of the squid will also work well. TECHNIQUE: Pull about 40, 2-foot pulls of line from your reel and troll very slowly much as you would for Lake Washington sockeye. You want your dodger barely swinging side to side, and a line angle not shallower than 45 degrees. With 6 or 8 ounces of lead, that should put you down at 30 to 40 feet and right where they live. Once you know you're into fish, a sporty alternative is to drift, rig light tackle and work Buzz Bombs or any of the other salmon jigs in the smaller sizes. Select pinks, reds or orange colors, as with any other lure. Handle pinks lightly, since their mouth tissue is soft and many are lost when the hook simply pulls out. If everyone around you is landing fish and you're not, the experts say there are two causes: your leader is too long or you're trolling too fast. SAFETY: Humpy Hollow and the launch at Mukilteo State Park are open and directly in the path of prevailing southwesterly winds. Casual anglers particularly, and those with small, less seaworthy craft, should take care to listen to a reliable weather forecast, keep an eye on wind conditions at all times, stock floatation devices for everyone on board and not get too far from the park. COMMERCIAL SEASON: Indian commercial fishing will be ongoing during the run. The non-Indian commercial season hasn't been formally set, but best estimates are that it will be one or two days, probably around Aug: 21 or 22. But it's not. In an era of depressed salmon runs, restrictive and confusing regulations, fights over allocations, Indian fishing rights, pollution and other factors, it's nice to have a sport fishery where limits are often the rule rather than the exception. It's a fish that may not be as big as a chinook, nor as high on the gourmet list as a sockeye, but a fine, affordable, close-to-home recreational opportunity nevertheless. It brings crowds out of the woodwork, assumes a circus atmosphere and, if you don't give it a whirl, you're missing out on a good thing.
