No one had ever seen hats thrown on the ice for a goaltender before, but Don Blishen wasn't about to complain. After shutting out the Seattle Thunderbirds 4-0, Blishen was a bit shocked to see hats heading his way. "As a matter of fact I have never seen that before," Blishen smiled. "But that's OK, maybe we've started a new trend." Blishen, a backup for most of the season, deserved a tip of the fedora Wednesday night. A somber, funereal crowd of only 5,319 seemed resigned to the fact they were seeing Stu Barnes, Greg Spenrath, Olie Kolzig and Steve Jaques for the last time in the coliseum. But instead of a sad farewell, the Tri-City faithful came to life and rocked the place like seldom before. Now the series will return to the Tri-Cities Saturday night. From start to finish, the Americans played by far their best hockey of the playoffs. That didn't take much. Lost and presumed dead, the Americans appeared ready to mail in the results after losing Tuesday night 8-2 to fall behind the best-of-nine series 3-0. Unlike the previous three games where the Americans were guilty of stupid penalties and then watched Seattle's power play click 12 times in 33 opportunities, the Americans played with much more discipline. "They played excellent hockey," Seattle Coach Peter Anholt said. "They were still physical and capitalized on their chances and killed off any penalties they did get." While the Americans were fighting for their playoff lives, Anholt admitted his team might have had a letdown. "Maybe we thought it would be easy," Anholt said. "This shows that it's never easy in this building. I hope this brought us back to earth a little bit. Next time we'll prepare better and won't take anything for granted."
