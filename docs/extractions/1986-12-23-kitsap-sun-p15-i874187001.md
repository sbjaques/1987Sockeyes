"Over half of are Puget Sound fish. The rest are from the north Washington coast, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, maybe 10 to 15 percent from the Columbia River. Grays Harbor is heavily impacted." The Canadian harvest there has a double-whammy effect on Washington. First of all, it takes nearly 2 million fish that would, had they lived, return south where Washington sport and commercial fishermen could get a crack at them. Secondly, the place where it occurs is a mixed-stock area. That means that fish there consist of both strong stocks and weak ones, and runs that aren't big enough to withstand heavy fishing are hit just as hard, relatively speaking, as the stronger ones. That causes problems for Washington fish managers when the coho finally return home. The stocks remain mixed until they separate in terminal areas near the rivers, and in some cases Washington fishermen must refrain from fishing on strong stocks because they still are mixed with weak ones which already have been hammered by Canadians. To make matters worse, the Canadians exceeded their 1.75-million-fish ceiling near Vancouver Island this year by more than 400,000. Why? "Poor management on their part," said Cedergreen.
