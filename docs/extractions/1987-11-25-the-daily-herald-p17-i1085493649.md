The study estimates that more than 300,000 kokanee (landlocked sockeye salmon; sometimes called "sliver trout") remain alive in what's left of the the big reservoir after irrigation drawdown and a prolonged drought. Rimrock has long been recognized as Washington's premier kokanee lake. Bureau of Land Management officials estimated earlier this year, however, that Rimrock would be drawn down completely by this time, thus killing the reservoir's estimated population of three million fish. A small pool of 10,000 acre feet remains and State Wildlife Department biologist Jim Cummins in Yakima is delighted. The department netted and moved 100,000 kokanee to adjacent Clear Lake earlier In the year. With those fish ready to be transferred back to Rimrock, plus the ones left alive, Cummins says the resource has the spawning core necessary to achieve a relatively painless comeback.
