Chilliwack returns leading point producer Billy MacGillivray and talented winger Danny Skene, but coach Mike O'Brien is truly excited about his recruits. Among them are Joey Botskin, the top center in the Alberta Junior League and a draft pick of the Atlanta Braves, and his former linemate Mark Gagnon, who had 55 points in 27 games. Leading scorer Bob Moon is back for Kelowna, which placed second in the Interior last year. Among the new faces will be talented goalies Dallas Mix and Gord Dale and Curt Vernon, a winger who played for Spokane of the Western Hockey League. Merritt coach John "Butch" Tent has a dozen veterans back from a squad that was beaten by Vernon in the Interior playoff finals. Second-leading scorer Paul Gentes is back along with the strong line of center Warren Carter, and wings Dave Bond and Dave Zakoll, and the team's two goalies. At Penticton, first-year coach Gary Davidson started over with a team that had no returnees thanks to the financial problems that forced it to be disbanded after 27 games last season. But even with only one 20-year-old and an average of only 17 years, the Panthers have some e talented, including center Paul Kariya, who Davidson calls "unbelievable." If the Bellingham Ice Hawks were looking for a Junior A hockey league to dominate the first season of their existence, they sure picked the wrong one. The British Columbia Junior Hockey League is generally considered the best Junior A league in Canada, which means the best in the Western Hemisphere. probably the best Tier 2 league anywhere," said Ice Hawks coach Mike Collins. It will be hard for the BCJHL to top last season, when division winners New Westminster and Vernon eventually met in the Centennial Cup final, the Super Bowl of Canadian Junior A hockey. Those two powerhouses, which have dominated the league the past two years, are back, although with new clubs since many players graduated out of the junior league because of the age limit (20). But there are new franchises and new faces galore around the rest of the league. Bellingham, of course, took over the Ladner franchise. The Cowichan Valley Warriors became the Victoria Warriors. The Richmond Sockeyes became the Chilliwack Chiefs. And the Penticton Knights stayed in Penticton, but became the Panthers when new owners took over for a a a a a a a a franchise that disbanded in the middle of last season. In addition to making additions, the league also restructured its divisional setup. Bellingham joins -New Westminster, Nanaimo, Powell River and Victoria in the Coastal Division. Chilliwack joins Vernon, Kelowna, Merritt and Penticton to form the Interior Division. Teams will face divisional rivals 10 times each and teams in the other division four times. The defending champions get the early nod as favorites again, although none of the coaches expect a team like New Westminster, which was 52-3-41 last season and broke just a about every team record on the books. "It was one of the best Junior 2 teams ever put together,' said Royals coach Harvey Smyl, who was an assistant coach last season. With five returnees - including outstanding goalie Todd Jones and center Greg Hadden, who set the league record for shorthanded goals two years ago - the Royals have a solid nucleus. New Westminster also is notorious for its recruiting and with new players like big center Phil Esposito (no relation) and left winger John Forchuk, the Royals should be in the hunt again. Victoria finished sixth last year, but has 17 players back including Pat Hodgins, one of the top centers in the league. The Warriors will be bolstered by Doug Popadynec, a Victoria native who was a first-team all-star in Manitoba, and Lorne Hadley, an all-star at Merritt last season. Powell River could have an explosive attack with center Brent Riplinger, one of seven returnees from last year's third-place squad, and center Jay McNeill, who moved up from Junior B and scored four goals in the Paper Kings' last exhibition game.
