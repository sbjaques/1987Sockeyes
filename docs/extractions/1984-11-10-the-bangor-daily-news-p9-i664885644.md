"It's a great recruiting tool," said Walsh. "It has already had an impact. Every time I've made a phone call to our recruits in Canada, they've all mentioned reading it.' "The Hockey News is really big up in Canada," said freshman defenseman Stan Czenczek of Surrey, British Columbia. "It's the biggest hockey magazine in Canada. I started reading it when I was 9 or 10 years old. It's going to help recruiting a lot, especial-. ly in Canada, because the recruits will notice that freshmen are playing regularly.' Walsh said he doesn't believe in over-recruiting, which means freshmen will be given every opportunity to make immediate contributions. Czenczek later told Walsh that "every Canadian player's dream is to be featured on the cover of The Hockey News." "Just about every Canadian family subscribes to The Hockey News," observed senior defenseman Rene Comeault of Dominion City, Manitoba. "The least it (the story) will do is let kids know Maine has a hockey team." Comeault said he didn't know Maine had a hockey team until the latter stages of his high school career. "I didn't know Maine had a hockey team until last year," agreed freshman defenseman Dave Nonis of Burnaby, British Columbia. "It's good for the program to get exposure like this.' Nonis and Comeault said it gives potential scholarship players some background information on the hockey program, which will help them when they're deciding upon schools. The Hockey News is also widely read in the U.S. according to Minnesota natives Bruce Hegland and Jeff Kloewer. Maine goalie Ray Roy will be making his second collegiate start in Lewiston's Central Maine Youth Center Saturday night when he starts against Mount Allison of Sackville, New Brunswick. It will be a familiar surrounding for Roy, who starred at St. Dominic's High School in Lewiston. He earned the Maine Interscholastic Hockey League's Most Valuable Player award during his senior year as he posted a 2.86 goalsagainst average. As a UMO freshman, Roy started an exhibition game against Division II New England College of Henniker, N.H., and was beaten 4-2. Roy made 18 saves in the game. "I hope I can have a better showing than the one I turned in the last time," said the junior netminder, who is 1-4 with a 4.48 goals-against average and an .882 save percentage this season. "The last time, it was my first college game and was playing at home, so there was a lot of pressure on me. I let up few sloppy goals, goals on which I got a piece of the puck. I should have stopped them." Roy said Lewiston is a hockey town and he said he usually plays well in the Youth Center. But he admitted that the pressure did, indeed, get to him two years ago. "I don't let things like that bother me now," said Roy, who is anxious to atone for that performance on Saturday night.
