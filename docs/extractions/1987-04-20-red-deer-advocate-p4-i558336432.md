The Rustlers are not the best team in the West • the Richmond Sockeyes and Humboldt Broncos will fight for that honor. But Red Deer's reborn Alberta Junior Hockey League team might be the best loved. They earned the community's support by overcoming seemingly insurmountable odds, by playing entertaining hockey, and by being more than a little brash. (The fans, mistakenly, thought throwing trash would show that they, too, were brash - but that's another story.) The Rustlers and their fans proved a couple of points: junior hockey can still draw here and a team can be operated successfully on a tight budget, without dragging down investors. They kept this town enthralled for a wild month of playoff hockey. And they may just have sealed their own fate, by showing once and for all that Red Deer could support a major junior hockey franchise if given the chance. Other voices Never underestimate the staying power of a bad idea, especially one that serves a political cause. Take "comparable worth" - or pay equity, as it has become known the belief that jobs can be objectively judged for their social value. The drive to establish pay equity by governmental action has slipped from the front pages lately, but its supporters have hardly abandoned their fight. The latest sign of the movement's vitality is a report by the National Committee on Pay Equity, whose board of directors includes such venerable organizations as the League of Women Voters, the American Library Association and the YWCA. Funded by the Ford Foundation, the report claims to be the first to confirm "what has long been suspected by pay equity advocates: that race, ethnicity and sex are all significant factors in setting pay." Actually, the report proves no such thing. It merely documents a well known fact: Women and minorities tend to be concentrated in certain jobs that tend to pay less than other jobs. The report's authors believe this pattern clinches the case for widespread discrimination. If such bias is so widespread, they suggest, what's the alternative to wholesale adjustment of wage scales? As imperfect as the present system may be in allocating income, it is far more responsive than the bureaucratic solution favored by the pay-equity forces. - An editorial from Rocky Mountain News, Denver.
