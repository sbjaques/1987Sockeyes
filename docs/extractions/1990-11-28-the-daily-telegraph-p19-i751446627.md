SIR FRANK CLARINGBULL, the geologist and mineralogist who has died aged 79, was an innovative director of the Natural History Museum where he greatly improved the style of presentation of exhibits during his tenure from 1968 to 1976. The hugely popular Hall of Human Biology, which opened in 1977, came into being largely on his initiative. Earlier, in 1953, as keeper of mineralogy and head of the X-ray section, Claringbull had worked on the jawbones of a number of prehistoric animals - and an unmarked specimen from the skull of "Piltdown Man". Discovered in 1908 by Charles Dawson, a Hastings solicitor, the Piltdown Man was at first thought to be the "missing link" between and homo sapiens. The Piltdown bone was found to contain mainly gypsum, which absent specimens of soil from the site - had been produced when the various bones were "cooked". The infamous hoax was confirmed when Claringbull brought X-ray powder photography to bear in making his tests on the skull. Gordon Frank Claringbull was born on Aug 21, 1911, and educated at Finchley Grammar School and Queen Mary College, London University, where he took a First in Geology and wrote a doctoral thesis on rocks in Aberdeenshire. He joined the mineralogical department of the Natural History Museum in 1935 as an assistant keeper. 1939 he took time out from then Museum University to go and to study Birming- X- ray crystallography with the chemist, Prof Gordon Cox; and outbreak of war he was invited by Cox to join research team working at the University for the Ministry of Supply. In 1943 he followed Cox to the Special Operations Executive laboratory at Welwyn Garden City, where he spent the rest of the war years working on the development of sabo-

tage explosives. After the war Claringbull visited. the Nazi concentration at Belsen to seek out imprisoned German scientists for Military Intelligence, before returning to the Natural History Museum. During his keepership of the mineralogical department, he was over-run with visitors bringing many mineral pieces for his expert opinion. He was frequently forced to disillusion people who had brought back specimens from India, believing them be rubies. Occasionally he would also adjudge on fragments of meteorites, notably on the one that fell at Barwell, Leics, in 1965. According to Claringbull, this specimen, with its pieces weighing 88lb, was the biggest ever to fall on Britain the record was previously held by the 56lb-meteorite that fell in Yorkshire in 1795. As the director of the Natural History Museum, Claringbull controlled a staff of about 900 in five principal departments, in which he did much to break down the older conservative tradition.
