Sir Frank Claringbull

FRANK CLARINGBULL was, as Neil Chalmers has written [obituary, 26 November] , lively and enthusiastic in all he did, sometimes to the point of impatience, writes Sir Gordon Cox. He was also highly adaptable and very quick in the uptake. When he came to study X-ray crystallography with me in 1939 he assimilated that subject, still almost as much an art as a science,

with astonishing rapidity. Then, in the early part of the war, he was back in Birmingham, working with such understanding and vigour on problems arising in the manufacture of explosives that before he left the work in 1943 he had produced a dozen reports on topics ranging from the ignition of explosives by electric sparks to the properties of conducting bakelite. Claringbull was a realist who knew that the Director should be seen to lead the museum from the front, but he was a modest man whose pride was not so much in his own achievements as in those of the scholars and highly-skilled specialists on whom the museum's reputation rested. He also cared about details of their working conditions; it was typical that almost his first act on being appointed Director was to have all the fire'bells rung, thereby revealing, as no doubt he suspected, that they were inaudible to about a third of the staff.
