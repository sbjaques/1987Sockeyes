Times-Colonist news services A SPOKESMAN for the Edmonton Oilers said Tuesday that coach and general manager Glen Sather and former Soviet goaltending star Vladislav Tretiak discussed an assistant coaching job last summer, but only in a "light-hearted" way. Bill Tuele, director of public relations for the National Hockey League team, said in Toronto that Sather entertained Tretiak at his home last August while the former Soviet national team netminder was visiting Canada to promote his autobiography. Tuele said the matter was raised **in good-natured fashion" with Sather joking that having Tretiak on staff would help the Oilers recruit other Soviet players. A report published Tuesday said Tretiak wanted $250,000 a year to serve as assistant coach of the Oilers. The Montreal Gazette said Edmonton owner Peter Pocklington countered with three one-year contracts worth $150,000 apiece. Attempts to reach Pocklington were unsuccessful. The report, citing an unnamed Ottawa source, said Tretiak - with the blessings of his Soviet sports ministry superiors - approached Pocklington in August about the job. The Gazette said Pocklington's offer was hand-delivered to Tretiak by an Edmonton businessman with links to the Soviet. The report also says Edmonton centre Wayne Gretzky put the former goalie in touch with his Edmonton-based business agent, Mike Barnett. Barnett said: "I don't care to comment. Anything further would have to be a statement on behalf of and from the Oilers or the Soviet sports committee."

Biletnikoff, who turns 45 later this month, spent 14 years with the BUS Raiders. The 6-foot-1, 190-pound flanker caught 589 passes - seventhbest all-time for 8,974 yards and 76 touchdowns. The native of Erie, Pa., caught 40 or more passes for 10 straight seasons, and his 70 catches for 1,167 yards and 10 touchdowns in 19 -season games were records at the time of his retirement. He played in three AFL and five AFC championship games, as well as in the 1968 and 1977 Super Bowls. Biletnikoff, now an assistant coach with the Calgary Stampeders, caught four passes for 79 yards to set up three Oakland scores in a 32-14 victory over Minnesota in the 1977 Super Bowl and was named the game's most valuable player. Page, a 42-year-old native of Canton, home of the Hall of Fame, in 1971 became the first defensive performer to be named NFL most valuable player. He was the NFC's defensive player of the year four times and was an all-league choice nine consecutive years. A graduate of Notre Dame, Page was the Vikings' second pick in the first round of the first combined AFLNFL draft in 1967. He played 238 games, all but three as a starter, for the Vikings and Bears. He played in 16 playoff games and four Super Bowls. Unofficial statistics show Page blocked 28 kicks and had 164 sacks during his 15 seasons. In 1976, he had a career high 21½ sacks. Ham, 39, missed only four games in his first 10 seasons in the NFL. The outside linebacker from Penn State finished a 12-year career with 25½ sacks, 21 fumble recoveries and 32 interceptions. New members of the Hall of Fame are elected annually by a 29-member board comprising media representatives from every league city and the president of the Pro Football Writers of America.
