by Mike McQuillan MetroValley News Service The New Westminster Royals have been the best in the B.C. Junior Hockey League's Coastal Division for the past two years. This season the club sits in the first place again, but you can't tell that from the crowds. Club owner Bruce Taylor paints a pretty picture when he claims the club gets 350 to 400 fans per game. The numbers are likely closer to 250 or 300. The whole experience flies directly in the face of his sportsbusiness philosophy. Winners bring in the fans, right? Wrong. "Let's face it," Taylor says with resignation, "winning teams don't fill seats." And that may possibly be the hardest lesson he has learned the past three years. A long-time benefactor of junior A hockey, Taylor has had shares in two other clubs. In his six years as an owner, Taylor's teams can boast s six coastal division titles, three BCJHL championships and one Centennial Cup (1987 with Richmond Sockeyes). But winning, for Taylor, has been confined to the ice. Fickle fans, a plethora of Junior B teams, little interest from Vancouver's electronic and daily print media or overworked hockey dads - take your choice, they're all possible reasons for the current financial predicament facing metropolitian Vancouver's remaining Junior A hockey team. Other Lower Mainland junior teams like the Richmond Sockeyes, Burnaby Bluehawks (formerly of Junior A league), Ladner Peguins and Nor West Caps have all hung up their jerseys over the past 20 years. Only New West survives today and that reign could be ending, if help, in the form of fans, doesn't come soon. "I think the writing is on the wall," says Taylor. "We have to do something and I'm not sure what it would be right now." Currently, eight junior B teams compete for the hockey fan dollar across the Lower Mainland. They may only attrat 300 or so fans per game, but that means fewer fans for Royals to draw on, he says. "Every place the Junior A's have moved out of, the Junior B's have moved into." With Junior A operating costs many times higher than Junior B franchises, those missing fans translate into two years of losses for the Royals. Taylor would not say how much the club has lost since 1988 but did confirm they had always operated in the red. The community press has been a great promotional tool for New West but, Taylor says, the problem with that is they serve one community. He needs to get his message out ot fans across the Lower Mainland. Vancouver's two daily newspapers aren't interested in the team until they get to the playoffs. "'They don't mind covering it a little bit but they don't want to be forced into covering it all the time. I think that's a big insult to the sport," says Taylor.
