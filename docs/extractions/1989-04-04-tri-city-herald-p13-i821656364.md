AMERICANS 5,591 watched the Winter Hawks take Game 7 in the best-ofnine Western Division semifinal with the continuing defensive effort that few expected from Portland in the playoffs. Portland's reward is the survivor of the Kamloops-Victoria series in another best-of-nine tussle for the Western Division championship. After falling behind early 3-0, the Americans staged an admirable turnaround to come within 3-2. It took Hamish Black's goal with 11:52 left to drive the spike through the Americans' cause and give Portland its 4-2 lead. "You can't take anything away from Tri-Cities," said Hamish Black. "They have a good hockey team, and they'll be back next year. Portland goalie Byron Dafoe, who worked the net in every game from start to finish, added, "They came out with a lot of juice in the first period and kept it going in the second." While Dafoe weathered those storms, his Tri-City counterparts - Olaf Kolzig and Frank Furlan - didn't. Kolzig faced only three shots before allowing the opening goal to Hamish Black, his sixth of the series, which came 7:40 into the first period. Furlan relieved Kolzig at that point and was victimized on the first shot he faced - a deflection by Brent Fleetwood at 9:37. Olver yanked Furlan after Portland's Greg Leahy was left unattended at the side of the net to tap in a power-play goal at 11:00 for a 3-0 Winter Hawk lead. Furlan faced only two shots, and both beat him for goals. The Americans squandered what initially appeared to be their best chance to make a game of it about a minute later when they wound up with a 5-on-3 man advantage for 1:48. The apprehensive, shocked, yet appreciative expression on goalie Frank Furlan's face when he was the first to hear the coliseum cheers is something that is as fresh today as it was when it happened about four months ago. Who will be unable to remember the dramatic overtime victory over the Seattle Thunderbirds that historic night? Who won't smile when they think of the Americans' two playoff victories at home? Or talk about Sean LeBrun's courage to suit up only six days after breaking his wrist? Who will soon be able to push from their memory the exquisite grace and speed of a player as talented as Stu Barnes? Or the acrobatic saves of top-notch goalies Furlan and Olie Kolzig? Or the steely eyed determination of defensemen Steve Jaques or Rob Krauss? And who will soon forget the remarkable; uncritical love affair that developed between the Tri-Cities and the Americans? It was such an infatuation that even Coach John Olver privately expressed concerns that it became difficult to motivate the team because of the constant adulation even when the team played poorly. Will the love affair continue? Probably. But as Portland Coach Ken Hodge said recently, Tri-City fans soon will become more sophisticated and demanding. One day soon a hat trick may be old hat.
