In 1939, Claringbull spent several months in the Chemistry Department of Birmingham University for the determination of crystal structure under Dr E.G. Cox, now Sir Gordon Cox. After the outbreak of war, he returned to Birmingham, having been seconded to the Ministry of Supply to work with Professor Cox on work which involved the application of the methods of mineralogy, chemistry and X-ray crystallography to the study of certain explosives. When Professor Cox was called to another appointment, Claringbull took over the leadership of the team until 1943, when he rejoined Cox to undertake special scientific duties for

the War Office connected with Combined Operations. He returned to the Natural History Museum in 1945 and was appointed Keeper of Mineralogy. The post -war period was one of significant developments in the fields of analytical and other techniques used in the characterisation of minerals and Claringbull was responsible for ensuring that the Department of Mineralogy was equipped with instrumental means of analysis, including the electron microprobe. Claringbull was appointed Director of the Museum in 1968 at a time of awakening interest in Britain in the educational and cultural role of museums. FRANK CLARINGBULL will be remembered as a fine geologist who made distinguished contributions in the field of X-ray crystallography and as an innovative Director of the British Museum (National History). Claringbull was born in 1911, and was educated at Finchley Grammar School and then at Queen Mary College, London, where he was awarded Ph D for his study of igneous rocks in the Balmoral area. He joined the staff of the Museum in 1935, and continued to work there, except for the war years, until his retirement in 1976. first duties were the care of the rock collection and, with Dr W. Campbell Smith, the systematic study of rocks.
