"The basis of first aid is to do the first treatment and then get them off to a doctor and that's the end of your commitment. "But in sports. first aid, our commitment is to get to the person, do the first aid, and look towards putting the person back on the field as quickly as possible," explained Greg Moro, a 17-year veteran of sports first aid who is one of the instructors for the Sport Medicine Council of B.C.'s SportsAid program. *We look at the long term rather than the short term," he continued. "*A normal first aid person gets a person for a short period of time and they never see them again, where we have a person and we see them all the way through their recovery." Part of following an athlete's recovery is the sports aider recognizing his limitations and referring the injured person to the hospital, family doctor or physiotherapist. Moro added, "Sports first aiders know a bit more about how the body reacts to injury so we . . . know what to do along with the icing (of certain injuries) so that healing takes place. "I think we look at the overall picture where the first aid person just looks at the start of the picture." None of this, however, is meant to criticize conventional first aid practices, stresses Moro, who has an industrial first aid "A ticket" and practices conventional and sports first aid at the Exhibition Park race track in Vancouver. (He has also served as a trainer for the Vancouver Whitecaps, Nanaimo Clippers Junior A Tier 2 hockey team, this year's Centennial Cup Champion Richmond Sockeyes, last year's B.C. Winter Games, and the Mac's Super Series hockey tournament for the last three years; he was also the head trainer at UBC last year.) Moro said it should simply be recognized that people with conventional first aid training have limitations when it comes to sport-related injuries. He offered as an example a soccer tournament where both regular first aid attendants and sports aiders were on duty. During the course of a match, a player went down with a twisted ankle and was attended by both "aiders." The conventional first aid attendant recommended splinting the leg and sending the player to emergency. But the sports aider iced the injury and taped the ankle for support and the player went back into the match a little later. Ice is a key ingredient to immediate and long-term treatment of most sports-related injuries as part of the "RICE" system. That is, 1 rest, ice, compression and elevation. Rest simply means putting the injured part at rest while ice and elevation are used together for the same purpose.
