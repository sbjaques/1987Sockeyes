NEW YORK (AP) - Commissioner Gary Bettman got approval from the NHL Board of Governors on Friday for league players to participate in the 1998 Olympics at Japan, and he gained an extra two years of labor peace in the bargain. A few details involving the world championships and Canada Cup competition still were to be worked out with the International Ice Hockey Federation and the International Olympic Committee, but it appeared the NHL participation at Nagano was set. NHL senior vice president and chief operating officer Steve Solomon attended Friday's meeting of the IIHF in Budapest, Hungary. "There are some items still open, some details to be solved," IIHF spokesman Kimmo Leinonen said. As part of the Olympic agreement, both the league and NHL Players Association agreed to waive the reopener clause in their Collective Bargaining Agreement, reached after an acrimonious 105-day lockout that disrupted last season. The original deal allowed either side to reopen the contract after the 1997-98 season. Without it in place, the current agreement stretches until the year 2000. "All sides have given up reopeners," Bettman said. "Both sides concluded it made sense in the context of building the sport. If the Olympics are as beneficial as we think they'll be, let's take advantage of it. It says a lot about how relations between the players association and the league have evolved in the last year. "There's nothing wrong with labor peace." Bettman and Bob Goodenow, head of the players association, canceled plans to fly to Budapest late Friday. Goodenow said the Olympic participation was not a done deal because of details still being worked on in Hungary. "There's still work to be done," he said. "If everything falls in place, we've agreed to the reopener." Norman Green, owner of the Dallas Stars, said waiving the reopener clause was a positive for both sides. "We've been talking about Olympic involvement for 20 years," he said. "Finally, it's been accomplished.'
